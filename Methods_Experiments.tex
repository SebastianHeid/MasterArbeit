\section{Block Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:MethodsBlockImportanceAnalysis}
Identifying the least important parts of the model, which can be compressed with minimal impact on the final model performance, is crucial for effective compression strategies. In the following, various block selection criteria are first presented, followed by selection algorithms tackling the challenge of identifying subsets of blocks for simultaneous compression.
\label{sec:BlockImportanceAnalyses}
\subsection{Block Selection Criteria}
Three different block selection criteria, namely magnitude-based, similarity-based, and metric-based selection, are presented. They are investigated in Sec. \ref{sec:ExpPixArtBlockSelectionCriterias} for PixArt-$\Sigma$.
\subsubsection{Magnitude-Based Selection}
\label{sec:MagnitudePruning}
In magnitude pruning \cite{han2015learning,filters2016pruning,lee2020layer}, the magnitudes of the weights within individual model components are computed to serve as a proxy for their relative importance. The underlying assumption is that small weight magnitudes correspond to a low overall impact on the model performance, implying that architectural components with lower weight magnitudes should be prioritized for pruning. The aggregated magnitude $M_i$ of a Transformer block $B_i$ is defined as the total Frobenius norm of its constituent weight matrices $W \in B_i$. To account for the multiple sub-layers within a block, the block-wise magnitude is computed as
\begin{equation} 
	M_i = \sqrt{\sum_{W \in B_i} \|W\|_F^2} \quad \text{with} \quad \|W\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_j \sum_k |w_{jk}|^2}.
\end{equation}
 Consequently, the block with the smallest magnitude $M_s = \min_i(M_i)$ is identified as the primary candidate for compression.

\subsubsection{Similarity-Based Selection}
\label{sec:CKA}
Another approach for identifying redundant model components is to compare their input and output representations \cite{men2024shortgpt}. If the transformation between the input and output is marginal, indicating that the specific component performs an approximate identity mapping, the model part is considered a candidate for compression. To quantify the similarity between the input and output features of a Transformer block, the centered kernel alignment (\gls{CKA}) metric \cite{kornblith2019similarity, pons2024effective} is employed. Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$ denote the input and $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$ the output features to be compared. First, the kernels $K$ and $L$ need to be chosen, which, in the linear case, correspond to the inner products of the input  $K=XX^T$ and the output $L=YY^T$. The final \gls{CKA} score is then given by 
\begin{equation}
	\text{CKA}(K,L) = \frac{\text{HSIC}(K,L)}{\sqrt{\text{HSIC}(K,K) \text{HSIC}(L,L)}} \quad.
\end{equation}
This calculation utilizes the Hilbert-Schmidt independence criterion (\gls{HSIC}) \cite{gretton2005measuring} defined as $\text{HSIC}(K,L) = \frac{1}{(n-1)^2}\text{tr}(KHLH)$ \cite{davari2022reliability}, where $H=I - \frac{1}{n} 11^T$ is the centering matrix to ensure invariance to mean shifts.

\subsubsection{Metric-Based Selection}
\label{sec:MetricBasedSelection}
A third approach for identifying the least important model components involves compressing all components, such as each Transformer block, independently and evaluating the resulting model configurations using image quality metrics on a small reference dataset (Fig. \ref{fig:MethodBlockImportance}). The reference dataset contains images generated by the original model using the same prompts and the same seed which were used for the compressed models. Specifically, let $\{B_1, \dots, B_L\}$ denote the individual blocks of a Transformer-based model architecture. Each block is compressed separately resulting in $L$ distinct model configurations $\{\{\hat{B}_1, \dots, B_L\}$, $\dots$, $\{B_1, \dots, \hat{B}_i, \dots,  B_L\}$, $\dots$,$\{B_1, \dots, \hat{B}_L\}\}$ where $\hat{B}_i$ indicates that the $i^\text{th}$ block is compressed. For each model configuration, the metrics are computed. In this work, the \gls{CMMD} and \gls{CLIP}-score are employed to rank the importance of the individual model components. While the \gls{CLIP}-score is used to evaluate semantic prompt-image coherence, \gls{CMMD} serves as a measure of general image quality and distributional fidelity. Since the \gls{CMMD} and the \gls{CLIP}-score produce values in different ranges and have opposing optimization directions (minimization versus maximization), a separate ranking of the blocks is created for each metric, where $r_{\text{CMMD},i}$ and  $r_{\text{CLIP},i}$ are the corresponding ranks of block $B_i$. The final importance score is obtained by computing the aggregated rank $r_{\text{final},i}$ as
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{final},i} = r_{\text{CMMD},i} + r_{\text{CLIP},i} \quad.
	\label{eq:combined_importance}
\end{equation}



In the importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive compression approach (Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression}) only the \gls{CMMD} value was leveraged to rank the Transformer blocks. In this approach, each block is individually compressed by a fixed ratio $c_\text{fixed}$. As this method compresses blocks to varying degrees across multiple iterations, applying $c_\text{fixed}$ leads to different numbers of reduced parameters $\Delta P$. Therefore, the \gls{CMMD} values are normalized by the number of discarded parameters, i.e. $\frac{\text{CMMD}_i}{\Delta P}$, to ensure fair comparison. The normalized \gls{CMMD} values are leveraged to quantify the degradation of image quality per reduced parameter count and to rank the blocks accordingly.  Note that the \gls{CLIP}-score was omitted in this setting to obtain a single, easily interpretable function. This approach is also used for all experiments with FLUX.1 {[dev]} in Sec. \ref{sec:ExpFlux}.

\subsection{Block Selection Algorithms}
The necessity of block selection algorithms arises from a specific challenge in metric-based model compression. The importance of individual model components, e.g. Transformer blocks, is typically determined in isolation, meaning only one block is modified, while the remaining parts of the model are held fixed during evaluation (Fig. \ref{fig:MethodBlockImportance}).  However, in practical applications, several components are compressed simultaneously. It cannot necessarily be assumed that the simultaneous parameter reduction of components with the lowest individual impact will yield the optimal combination for preserving image quality. In the following, methods to select the best subset of model components for compression are described, and subsequently evaluated in Sec. \ref{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis}.
\label{sec:BlockSelectionAlgo}
\subsubsection{Greedy Block Selection}
\label{sec:GreedyBlockSelection}
The greedy algorithm is applied in conjunction with the metric-based selection criterion. It is an iterative procedure in which the importance of all currently uncompressed blocks is determined to identify and compress the least critical one. Following this step, the block importance is re-evaluated for all remaining uncompressed blocks. Concretely, let the model consist of $L$ Transformer blocks $B=\{B_1, \dots, B_L\}$. 
In the first iteration, the metrics are computed for all $L$ possible model configurations $\mathcal{M}^{(1)} = \{ (B \setminus \{B_i\}) \cup \{\hat{B}_i\} \mid 1 \leq i \leq L \}$ and the optimal block $B_j$ is selected according to the ranking. Let $\hat{B}_j$ denote the compressed version of block $B_j$.
 In the second iteration, the metrics are re-computed for all $L-1$ remaining blocks
 $\mathcal{M}^{(2)} = \{ (B \setminus \{B_i, B_j\}) \cup \{\hat{B}_i, \hat{B}_j\} \mid 1 \leq i \leq L, i \neq j \}$.  For the $n^\text{th}$ iteration $L-(n-1)$ uncompressed blocks remain. Let $B_\text{comp}$ denote the set of all previously compressed blocks then the potential candidates for compression are given by  $\mathcal{M}^{(n)} = \{ (B \setminus (B_\text{comp} \cup  \{B_i\})) \cup \hat{B}_\text{comp} \cup \{\hat{B}_i\} \mid B_i \in B \setminus B_\text{comp} \}$.
 This process is repeated until the desired number of blocks to be compressed has been identified.

\subsubsection{Black-Box Optimization with Optuna}
\label{sec:Optuna}
Optuna \cite{akiba2019optuna} is an open-source hyperparameter optimization framework that primarily leverages a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator \cite{bergstra2011algorithms}, a Bayesian optimization algorithm, to search the space of all possible parameter settings, which in this work corresponds to the discrete combinatorial space of block compression configurations. It partitions the observed hyperparameter configurations into promising and non-promising candidates based on a user-defined objective function. By modeling these two groups using probability density functions, it draws a new set of hyperparameters that belongs to the promising group  with high probability. Through this informed search, the global optimum is theoretically expected to be located much faster than with non-adaptive methods such as random or grid search. For an in-depth description of the Optuna algorithm the reader is referred to the original paper \cite{akiba2019optuna} as a more detailed explanation would go beyond the scope of this work.

\section{Model Compression Strategies}
\label{sec:StructuralCompressionStrategy}
In the literature, two dominant approaches are utilized for compressing \gls{DiT}s. One approach involves structural pruning of individual model parts \cite{flux1-lite,flux_mini_2024}, while the other one replaces model parts by smaller modules such as linear layers \cite{FastFlux,PPCL}. In this Master's Thesis, the complete pruning of Transformer blocks (Sec. \ref{sec:MethodBlockRemoval}) is investigated as a baseline. Furthermore, instead of replacing model parts, the application of low-rank approximations of projection matrices within the Transformer blocks is investigated realized via truncated \gls{SVD}. For \gls{SVD}-based compression, a distinction is made between compressing blocks only once or iteratively compressing them multiple times (Sec. \ref{sec:IterativeRepeatedSVDCompression}). Finally, \gls{GRASP} \cite{GRASP}, a more advanced \gls{SVD}-based compression technique is investigated as a proof-of-concept for its transferability from \gls{LLM}s to \gls{DiT}s.


%There are two primary strategies in model pruning. On the one hand, individual model components can be removed entirely, or, on the other hand, they can be compressed, e.g. via low-rank approximation. In Transformer-based architectures like PixArt-$\Sigma$ and FLUX.1 {[dev]}, a natural choice is to partition the model according to individual Transformer blocks. Therefore, any reference to "model parts" in the following sections specifically denotes these individual Transformer blocks. Both strategies and their respective variants are described in the section below. 
\subsection{Block Pruning}
\label{sec:MethodBlockRemoval}
In structural model pruning, entire functional components of a model are eliminated \cite{Bksdm, Laptop, Koala} while others that are considered highly relevant for the model's performance are maintained. Concretely, if a Transformer block $B_i$ is removed, the output features of block $B_{i-1}$ (denoted as $x_{i-1}$) are directly passed to block $B_{i+1}$

\begin{equation}
x_{i+1} = B_{i+1}(x_{i-1}) \quad.
\end{equation}
Due to the consistent dimensionality of the hidden features across the model's depth, no additional dimension adaptation is required. A schematic sketch of this procedure is provided in Fig. \ref{fig:CompRemScheme} (right).

\subsection{SVD-Based Compression}
\label{sec:SVDCompression}
Singular value decomposition (\gls{SVD}) \cite{zhang2015singular} factorizes a matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ into three matrices $U, \Sigma, V$ such that 
\begin{equation}
W = U \Sigma V^T
\end{equation}
where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ are orthogonal matrices and $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ is a diagonal matrix. The entries of $\Sigma$ are referred to as singular values and are sorted in descending order $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \sigma_3 \dots \geq \sigma_p$ with $p = \min(n,m)$. The magnitude of each singular value serves as a proxy for the importance of the corresponding column of $U$ and row of  $V^T$.

 In model compression, truncated \gls{SVD} is utilized to approximate weight matrices by retaining only the $r<p$ largest singular values, such that $U_\text{red} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$, $V_\text{red} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$ and $\Sigma_\text{red} \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$, thereby reducing the total parameter count depending on the choice of $r$. Specifically, the matrix $W$ is approximated by two matrices $A=U_\text{red}\sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}}$ and $B=\sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}}V_\text{red}^T$. Given a feature vector $\mathbf{x}$, the standard transformation $\mathbf{x}_\text{next} = W\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b} $ is replaced by
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}_\text{next} =AB \mathbf{x} +  \mathbf{b}
\end{equation}
where $ \mathbf{b}$ is the bias term, which remains unchanged.
Rather than defining a fixed rank $r$, it is more practical to specify the percentage $c$ of parameters to be discarded, referred to as the compression ratio. For a weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$, the initial number of parameters is given by $P_\text{orig} = n \times m$. The target number of parameters is computed by $P_{\text{targ}} = P_{\text{orig}} \times (1.0 - c)$. Consequently, the target rank is determined as 
\begin{equation}
	r_{\text{targ}} = \max\left(1, \text{int}\left(\frac{P_{\text{targ}}}{n+m}\right)\right) \quad.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Iterative SVD-Based Compression}
\label{sec:IterativeRepeatedSVDCompression}
In the final compression setup (Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression}), individual Transformer blocks are compressed and subsequently fine-tuned multiple times using truncated \gls{SVD}.

 Initially, an original weight matrix $W$ is decomposed into matrices $A$ and $B$ such that $W \approx AB$. During the subsequent fine-tuning, the matrices $A$ and $B$ are updated to $A_t$ and $B_t$. Consequently, the relation $W \approx A_tB_t$ does not necessarily hold with respect to the initial matrix $W$, as the model adapts to the low-rank constraint. Therefore, if the Transformer block is to be compressed further, the current effective weight matrix $W_t = A_t B_t$ is first reconstructed, followed by the application of the \gls{SVD} algorithm to obtain $W_t = U^{(2)} \Sigma^{(2)} V^{(2)^T}$. Similar to the initial step, after choosing a further reduced rank, the new smaller matrices $A^{(2)}$ and $B^{(2)}$ are constructed via $A^{(2)} = U^{(2)}_\text{red} \sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}^{(2)}}$  and $B^{(2)} =  \sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}^{(2)}}V_\text{red}^{(2)^T}$.  By repeating this procedure, reconstructing $W^{(j)}_t = A^{(j)}_t B^{(j)}_t$, choosing a new, smaller rank and obtaining the new $A^{(j+1)} = U^{(j+1)}_\text{red} \sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}^{(j+1)}}$  and $B^{(j+1)} =  \sqrt{\Sigma_\text{red}^{(j+1)}}V_\text{red}^{(j+1)^T}$, a Transformer block can be gradually compressed across multiple iterations.


\subsection{GRASP}
\label{sec:GRASP}
The method named Gradient-Based Retention of Adaptive Singular Parameters \gls{GRASP} was originally proposed for compressing \gls{LLM}s \cite{GRASP}. It is based on the idea of exploiting the inherent low-rank structure of weight matrices within \gls{LLM}s. Crucially, the authors hypothesized that the standard \gls{SVD} approach where the singular values are selected solely based on their magnitude is suboptimal. They argue that even small singular values may be vital for the model's performance and should be preserved. Consequently, they proposed a gradient-based criterion instead of a magnitude-based selection for the singular values to keep.

 In this work, the \gls{GRASP} method is transferred to image generation models to demonstrate its applicability to FLUX.1 {[dev]}. First, the candidate blocks for compression are identified using the method described in Sec. \ref{sec:BlockImportanceAnalyses}. Next, the projection matrices within these blocks are decomposed via \gls{SVD}. Afterwards, all parameters are frozen and only the singular values are treated as trainable parameters for gradient computation. For computing the gradients, a calibration dataset is utilized consisting of 200 images. For each image, the gradients with respect to each singular value are computed and their absolute values are accumulated over all timesteps $t \in \{1,\dots,1000\}$ using the standard flow matching loss. Finally, the gradients are averaged over the calibration dataset to obtain a final estimate of the importance of each singular value. Note that gradients are accumulated for scoring but the singular values themselves are not updated. Ultimately, instead of retaining the $k$ largest singular values by magnitude, the $k$ singular values with the largest accumulated gradient magnitudes are selected for the low-rank approximation of the original weight matrix.

Let $j$ denote the number of compressions already performed for the corresponding weight matrix. To compress it a $(j+1)^\text{th}$ time, first the full matrix based on the finetuned matrices $A^{(j)}$ and $B^{(j)}$, namely $W^{(j)} = A^{(j)}B^{(j)}$ is constructed. \section{Knowledge Distillation Loss Functions}
\label{sec:MethodKDLoss}
The general learning objective for knowledge distillation, as described in Eq. \ref{eq:KnowledgeDistillationLoss}, is given by
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L_\text{Task}} + \lambda_\text{OutKD} \mathcal{L_\text{OutKD}} + \lambda_\text{FeatKD} \mathcal{L_\text{FeatKD}} \quad.
\end{equation}
Sec. \ref{sec:ExpKDLoss} investigates the importance of the individual components of this loss function. It has been proven beneficial to normalize the loss of the individual features due to differences in their magnitude. The normalization details are discussed in Sec.  \ref{sec:KDLNormalization}. Furthermore, to compute the feature loss, Sec. \ref{sec:FeatureMappingForLossComputation} provides an overview of the feature mapping between the student and teacher models.

\subsection{Feature Loss Normalization}
\label{sec:KDLNormalization}
It is important to note that the features of different blocks may have varying magnitudes. Consequently, previous research \cite{Laptop} demonstrated that applying a normalization to these features is beneficial. Therefore, after computing the difference between the teacher and student features, a normalization is applied to ensure that the individual blocks contribute equally to $\mathcal{L_\text{FeatKD}}$. The normalization scheme adopted in this work is taken from \cite{Dense2MoE}.

  Let $T_l$ and $S_l$ denote the features of the teacher and the student corresponding to Transformer block $l \in \{1,\dots,L\}$. For a batch size of $b$, each feature tensor has the dimensions $(b,d_l)$ where $d_l$ is the total number of flattened features. The $i^{\text{th}}$ sample in the batch for layer $l$ is denoted as $T_{i,l}$ and $S_{i,l}$, respectively.  
First, the \gls{MSE} is computed between the teacher and the student features 
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{i,l} = \frac{1}{d_l} \| T_{i,l} - S_{i,l}\|_2^2 \quad.
\end{equation}
Next, the \gls{RMS} of the teacher features is computed, serving as a measure of feature magnitude
\begin{equation}
 \eta_{i,l} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d_l} \|T_{i,l}\|^2_2} \quad.
\end{equation}
%Additionally, the loss is scaled by the magnitude of the task loss $\mathcal{L}_{i,\text{Task}}$. The normalization factor is then given by

The overall normalization factor $w_{i,l}$ is then constructed by combining a loss balancing term, which scales the feature loss ${\mathcal{L}_{i,l}}$ relative to the task loss $\mathcal{L}_{i,\text{Task}}$, and a feature magnitude normalization term

\begin{equation}
	w_{i,l} = \underbrace{\left( \frac{\mathcal{L}_{i,\text{Task}}}{\mathcal{L}_{i,l} + \epsilon} \right)}_{\text{Loss Balancing}} \cdot \underbrace{\left( \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{L} \eta_{i,k}}{L \cdot \eta_{i,l} + \epsilon} \right)}_{\text{Magnitude Normalization}}
\end{equation}
where a small $\epsilon$ is added to the denominators to prevent division by zero. The final feature loss is computed as follows
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}_\text{FeatKD} = \frac{1}{b}\sum_{i=1}^{b} \left(\frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^{L} w_{i,l} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{i,l}\right) \quad.
\end{equation}
Note that this normalization applies not only to $\mathcal{L}_\text{FeatKD}$ but also to $\mathcal{L_\text{OutKD}}$ which compares the final output of the teacher with the student's output.


\subsection{Feature Mapping for Loss Computation}
\label{sec:FeatureMappingForLossComputation}
The assignment of learning signals based on intermediate features differs significantly between the block pruning and the \gls{SVD} block compression approach (Fig. \ref{fig:LossRemovalCompression}). In the \gls{SVD}-based compression approaches, the intermediate features of every student block $S_l$ are directly compared with the features of the corresponding teacher block $T_l$, for all $l \in \{1,\dots,L\}$, assuming the model consists of $L$ Transformer blocks. 

In contrast, the pruning approach necessitates a dynamic mapping strategy for selecting the correct intermediate features. If a block $B_l$ is removed, the features of the preceding student block $S_{l-1}$ are compared with the features of the teacher block $T_l$. This effectively forces block $B_{l-1}$ to approximate the output representations of the removed block $B_l$. Consequently, if several consecutive blocks $B_{l-i}$ to $B_l$ (where $i\geq1$) are removed, the student features $S_{l-(i+1)}$ are compared to $T_l$.

\section{Compression Schedule}
\label{sec:MethodPruningSchedule}
The compression schedule dictates when and to what extent the compression of individual model components is performed. The design of a compression schedule includes two dimensions. First, a temporal dimension determines whether target compression ratio is achieved in a single step or progressively over the course of several iterations. Second, a structural dimension specifies how the compression budget is allocated across the model components and whether they can be compressed multiple times in the case of an iterative approach. To explore these aspects,
three distinct compression schedules, namely one-shot (Sec. \ref{sec:MethodOneShotPruning}), progressive (Sec. \ref{sec:MethodProgressiveModelPruning}), and importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive (Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression}) compression, are investigated in this Master's thesis and presented in the following subsections.

\subsection{One-Shot Model Compression}
\label{sec:MethodOneShotPruning}
The standard approach for compressing Transformer-based diffusion and flow models \cite{Laptop, Koala, flux1-lite, flux_mini_2024} involves three main steps. First, the Transformer blocks $\{B_1, \dots, B_L\}$ are sorted according to their importance, e.g. using the \gls{CLIP}-score and \gls{CMMD} as importance proxies (Sec. \ref{sec:MetricBasedSelection}). Let $\{B^*_1, \dots, B^*_L\}$ denote the sorted blocks, where $B^*_1$ represents the least important one. Second, based on the target compression ratio of the total model, the number of blocks $k \leq L$ to be compressed is determined, and the blocks $\{B^*_1, \dots, B^*_k\}$ are modified based on the chosen compression strategy (Sec. \ref{sec:StructuralCompressionStrategy}). In this setup, every selcted block undergoes a uniform parameter reduction, e.g. compressed by the same ratio. Third, the reduced model is fine-tuned, e.g. using knowledge distillation (Sec. \ref{sec:BackgroundKnowledgeDistillation}), to recover its generative capabilities. This approach introduces a significant, abrupt structural alteration to the model, leading to a severe drop in initial performance which must be recovered. A schematic representation of this schedule is depicted in Fig. \ref{fig:OneShotCompressionScheme}.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/Methoden/OneShotCompressionScheme.png}
\caption[One-Shot Compression Schedule]{Illustration of the one-shot compression procedure. The importance of all blocks is determined, followed by the compression of the least important blocks to reach the target model size.}
	\label{fig:OneShotCompressionScheme}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Progressive Model Compression}
\label{sec:MethodProgressiveModelPruning}
In contrast to one-shot model compression, progressive model compression, e.g. \cite{ProgressiveDistillation}, iteratively applies the three-step procedure described above. In each iteration, the importance of previously unmodified blocks is re-evaluated, the number of blocks that need to be modified is determined, and the compressed model is fine-tuned. The crucial difference is that the number of parameters of the model reduced in a single iteration is significantly smaller than in the one-shot model compression approach. This leads to a gradual and less severe performance degradation in each iteration, which is potentially easier to recover from. Note that each Transformer block is compressed only once by the same amount. In any subsequent iteration, the block importance is re-assessed only for the remaining unmodified blocks, as the relative block ranking may have changed as a result of the fine-tuning. A schematic representation of this schedule is depicted in Fig. \ref{fig:ProgressiveCompressionScheme}.

 A key design choice in progressive model compression concerns the extent of parameter reduction per iteration. The amount of discarded parameters can either be set to a fixed value, or it can be chosen dynamically based on importance metrics, for example by defining a threshold $\text{CMMD}=0.1$. Fine-tuning is then initiated as soon as this threshold is exceeded after compressing an additional Transformer block. The latter approach is utilized as the standard setting (Sec. \ref{sec:DefaultSetup}) for all experiments. This typically implies that in early iterations a higher compression ratio is achieved than in later iterations.

\caption[Progressive Compression Schedule]{Illustration of the progressive compression procedure. In each iteration, only the remaining unmodified blocks are analyzed for their importance, followed by the compression of the least important ones. This process repeats until the target model size is reached, ensuring that each block is compressed at most once.}
	\label{fig:ProgressiveCompressionScheme}

\subsection{Importance-Aware Rank-Adaptive Progressive Model Compression}
\label{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression}
In this work, a new importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive compression schedule specifically designed for truncated \gls{SVD} is proposed. This approach advances standard progressive model compression by introducing a fourth step, namely determining individual compression ratios for each block during each iteration based on its task importance. 

Here, \textit{importance-aware} indicates that all blocks are ranked according to their relevance for the image generation process, similar to previous schedules. \textit{Rank-adaptive} signifies that the blocks are not compressed uniformly, as is common in standard progressive model reduction. Instead, varying \gls{SVD} ranks are applied based on their position in the importance ranking, such that less critical blocks are compressed more aggressively than important ones. Concretely, the assigned compression ratios linearly decrease with increasing block importance. Furthermore, rather than modifying each block only once, this method allows blocks to be compressed across multiple iterations. 

 The initial step of this proposed compression schedule aligns with the previous approaches. The block importance is determined by leveraging the \gls{CMMD}. However, as multiple compressions of the same Transformer block are possible, the \gls{CMMD} value is normalized by the reduction in parameter count to ensure a fair comparison. For instance, reducing an already compressed block by $c_\text{fixed} = 60\%$ saves fewer parameters than compressing a full Transformer block by the same percentage. Let $P_i$ $(i \in \{1, \dots, L\})$ denote the current number of parameters of block $B_i$. Then, the final importance score for each block $B_i$ is given by 
 \begin{equation}
 	s_i = \frac{\text{CMMD}_i}{P_i \cdot c_\text{fixed}}
 \end{equation}
where $\text{CMMD}_i$ is the metric obtained by evaluating the current model after compressing block $B_i$. Based on these scores, the blocks are sorted into a sequence $\{B^*_1, \dots, B^*_L\}$ where $B_1^*$ has the smallest importance score, representing the most favorable trade-off between performance degradation and parameter reduction.

Second, in a crucial departure from standard progressive compression, a linear compression ratio schedule is applied to determine an individual compression ratio for each block, wherein the least important block is compressed most, and blocks of higher importance less. Specifically, three parameters need to be specified by the user to compute the individual compression ratios $c_i$, namely the total parameter count to be reduced $N$ in the current iteration, the number of blocks to be compressed $k$ and the compression ratio of the least important block $c_1 = S$ which is referred to as the base compression ratio. To determine the compression ratios $c_i$ of the remaining blocks $B_i^* \in \{B^*_1, \dots, B^*_k\}$, the base compression ratio $S$ is decreased linearly by the decay rate $x$ according to the block's rank in the importance-sorted list. Therefore, the compression ratio for every block $B_i^*$ is given by
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:CompressionRatio}
	c_i = S - ((i-1) \cdot x)
\end{equation}
with $i \in \{1, \dots, k\}$. The goal is to identify $x$ based on the parameters $S$, $N$, and $k$. The total parameter reduction is computed by
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:TotalParam}
	N = \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i \cdot c_i
\end{equation}
where $P^*_i$ is the current parameter count of block $B_i^*$. To solve for $x$, Eq. \ref{eq:CompressionRatio} and Eq. \ref{eq:TotalParam} are combined

\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		& \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i (S - (i-1) \cdot x) = N \\
		\Leftrightarrow \quad & \sum_{i=1}^{k} (P^*_i \cdot S) - \sum_{i=1}^{k} (P^*_i \cdot (i-1) \cdot x) = N \\
		\Leftrightarrow \quad & \sum_{i=1}^{k} (P^*_i \cdot S) - N = x \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{k} (P^*_i \cdot (i-1))
	\end{split}
	\label{eq:linear_compression_derivation}
\end{equation}


resulting in the final equation for the linear decay rate
\begin{equation}
	x = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k}\left(P^*_i \cdot S \right) - N}{ \sum_{i=1}^{k}\left(P^*_i \cdot (i-1)  \right)} \quad.
\end{equation}
The input parameters $S$, $N$, and $k$ specified by the user must meet two conditions. First, $N \leq S \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i$, meaning the total parameter reduction must not exceed the amount removed if all blocks were compressed by the base ratio $S$. Otherwise, the linear decay rate $x$ would become negative, causing the compression ratio to increase for more important blocks, which would contradict the ranking strategy. Second, the compression ratio of the last block, $c_k$, must not be negative, which leads to the following condition
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		& 0 \leq c_k = S - (k-1) \cdot x \\
		\Leftrightarrow \quad & 0 \leq S - (k-1) \frac{S \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i - N}{\sum_{i=1}^{k}(P^*_i \cdot (i-1))} \\
		\Leftrightarrow \quad & N \geq S \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i - \frac{S \sum_{i=1}^{k}(P^*_i \cdot (i-1)) }{k-1} \quad.
	\end{split}
\end{equation}

Combining these constraints, a valid range for the total number of parameters to remove $N$ in one iteration based on the choice of $S$ and $k$ is obtained as
\begin{equation}
	S \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i - \frac{S \sum_{i=1}^{k}(P^*_i \cdot (i-1)) }{k-1}  \leq N \leq S \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{k} P^*_i \quad.
\end{equation}

The third step, after computing the compression ratios $c_i$, is to compress the model using truncated \gls{SVD} as described in Sec. \ref{sec:IterativeRepeatedSVDCompression}. The fourth and final step of the iteration is to fine-tune the compressed model to recover its generative capabilities. This procedure is then repeated until the desired model size is reached.

 A schematic representation of the entire procedure is depicted in Fig. \ref{fig:LinearCompressionScheme}, where steps three and four (model compression and fine-tuning) are summarized in the last element of the figure.
