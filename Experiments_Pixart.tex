\chapter{Experiments}
\label{sec:Experiments}
This chapter investigates low-rank model compression leveraging truncated \gls{SVD} on transformer blocks within the PixArt-$\Sigma$ and Flux.1-dev architectures. PixArt-$\Sigma$ is employed as an experimental baseline to evaluate various design choices in the pruning framework. Its relatively low parameter count enables rapid iteration and extensive ablation studies. Subsequently, the most effective strategies are applied to Flux.1-dev to assess their efficacy in high-parameter regimes. 
\section{PixArt-$\Sigma$ Pruning}
\label{sec:PixArtExp}

In the following sections, individual components of the compression framework are evaluated to identify the optimal configuration. To isolate the impact of each component, a default experimental setup is defined (Sec. \ref{sec:DefaultSetup}). In the subsequent experiments, only deviations from this default setting, e.g. different loss function, are explicitly mentioned to avoid repetition.

 The initial experiments investigate how to select the model components that contain the highest redundancy and are therefore most suitable for pruning (Sec. \ref{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis} and \ref{sec:PixartBlockSelectionAlgorithms}). Next,  two different pruning schedules (Sec. \ref{sec:OneShot_vs_Progrosseve_Pruning}), the loss function (Sec. \ref{sec:ExpKDLoss}), and  post-compression fine-tuning protocols (Sec. \ref{sec:fine-tuningProtocol}) are evaluated. Furthermore, compression strategies, namely complete block removal versus \gls{SVD}-based block compression are investigated (Sec. \ref{sec:CompressionStrategyPixart}). The focus then shifts entirely to \gls{SVD}-based compression to systematically analyze the sensitivity of transformer block components to low-rank approximation (Sec. \ref{sec:MLPAttnDiffCompRatios}), alongside compression schedules (Sec. \ref{sec:CompressionSchedulingPixart}). Finally, the found insights are summarized (Sec. \ref{sec:PixArtBestModel}) and leveraged in the following Sec. \ref{sec:ExpFlux} to compress Flux.1-dev.

For the quantitative evaluation of the experiments, the HPSv2 \cite{HPSv2} and GenEval \cite{ghosh2023geneval} benchmarks are utilized, as they serve different purposes and complement each other well. HPSv2 measures overall image quality while GenEval focuses on text-image alignment.


\subsection{Experimental Default Setup}
\label{sec:DefaultSetup}
This section defines the default configuration settings that are utilized in all experiments unless stated otherwise. It is important to note that this baseline configuration serves as a consistent starting point for the experiments and does not necessarily represent the final, optimal setting, which is derived later in this work. The baseline configuration comprises the following elements:

\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Block Selection Algorithm}: The greedy algorithm based on \gls{CMMD} and \gls{CLIP}-score was utilized, computed over 100 images.
		
	\item \textbf{Pruning Schedule}: The progressive compression strategy was applied. As soon as the \gls{CMMD} value exceeded 0.1 in the block selection algorithm, the model was retrained to recover its generation capabilities.
	
	\item \textbf{Learning Objective}: Knowledge distillation was utilized for fine-tuning the pruned models. Therefore, the learning objective is defined as the sum of the unnormalized feature and output distillation losses,  $\mathcal{L_\text{OutKD}} + \mathcal{L_\text{FeatKD}}$.
	
		\item  \textbf{Fine-tuning Protocol}: The recovery of the pruned model followed a structured three-stage fine-tuning protocol. First, for local stabilization, optimization was restricted to the block $B_{l-1}$ immediately preceding the removed block $B_l$ for one epoch (38k steps) on the LAION dataset. In cases where several blocks were removed simultaneously, all corresponding preceding blocks were updated while the rest of the network was frozen. Second, the complete model was optimized for an additional epoch on the LAION dataset. Third, for domain-specific refinement, the model was fine-tuned for two epochs (12.5k steps) on the LAION-PixArt dataset.  
		
		\item \textbf{Structural Compression Strategy}: Complete transformer block removal was employed to reduce the model size.
		
	\item \textbf{Training Datasets}: Training was performed sequentially starting on the LAION dataset followed by further training on the LAION-PixArt dataset. For each iteration, the models were trained for two epochs on each dataset.

	\item \textbf{Evaluation}: The pruned models were evaluated on the HPSv2 and GenEval benchmarks. The HPSv2 benchmark assesses overall image quality by predicting a human preference score, whereas the GenEval benchmark analyzes text-image alignment.
	\item \textbf{Training Hyperparameters}: All other training hyperparameters are summarized in Tab. \ref{tab:hyperparameterTraining}.

\end{itemize}

The implementation of all experiments builds upon the official Git repository for PixArt-$\Sigma$ \cite{pixartsigma_code}.

\subsection{Block Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis}
In structural pruning, the selection of components to be removed or compressed is essential to ensure that the resulting smaller model retains the highest possible image generation quality. Qualitatively, Fig. \ref{fig:PixArtBlockImportanceQualitatively} shows the effect of removing each transformer block from PixArt-$\Sigma$ individually on its image generation capabilities without subsequent fine-tuning. It is clearly visible that removing the first and last transformer blocks (blocks 1-4 and blocks 27-28) as well as block 10 exerts the most significant influence on overall image quality. This confirms the observation from previous work \cite{men2024shortgpt} that redundancies are primarily found in the middle layers of transformer-based models. To identify which blocks are most suitable for pruning, i.e., those that make minimal contribution to the overall image generation process, several different metrics can be employed. In this section, various approaches are investigated to determine the importance of the individual transformer blocks. First, traditional magnitude-based pruning and representational dissimilarity methods are evaluated, specifically by computing the centered kernel alignment score (\gls{CKA}) between the input and output of each transformer block. Moreover, these methods are verified against two standard performance metrics: \gls{CLIP}-score and \gls{CMMD}. 

 In practice, several transformer blocks are removed or compressed simultaneously. Due to block interdependencies, two different methods for identifying the best grouping of blocks are investigated. First, a greedy algorithm is applied, iteratively searching for the best next block to prune based on the already compressed model from previous iterations. Second, the block selection process is formulated as a hyperparameter search leveraging the Optuna framework.

\subsubsection{Block Selection Criteria}
\label{sec:ExpPixArtBlockSelectionCriterias}
In magnitude-based pruning strategies, the model components with the lowest averaged weight magnitude are considered less important for overall model performance. Interestingly, in PixArt-$\Sigma$, the weights of the initial layers, which have a substantial influence on the final image synthesis, exhibit the lowest magnitudes across the architecture (Fig. \ref{fig:PixArtBlockImportanceMetrics} (b)). This observation contradicts the assumption that low weight magnitudes equate to low impact. It seems that despite their small weight magnitude, the first layers act as important initial feature translators, having a massive influence on model performance. Furthermore, the weight magnitudes of the final layers are only marginally higher than those of the middle layers, while the middle layers' weight magnitudes exhibit a high degree of similarity. This makes a precise selection of blocks for removal based solely on this heuristic difficult. Consequently, using weight magnitude to identify redundant blocks within PixArt-$\Sigma$ is not a suitable criterion for structural pruning in this context.

The \gls{CKA} score is computed for every block and every sampling step across 100 example prompts taken from the LAION dataset. Subsequently, these scores are averaged across all samples and time steps, resulting in a mean similarity score $\mu_{CKA,i}$ for each individual PixArt-$\Sigma$ block. Averaging over the time steps should provide an estimate of the general importance of the block. However, the importance of a block at different time steps can vary drastically (Appx. A). A low value indicates that the input and output of a specific block differ significantly, suggesting a higher importance of the block. 

Fig. \ref{fig:PixArtBlockImportanceMetrics} (a) shows the transformation intensity $1-\mu_{CKA,i}$ for every block, a higher value signifies a more substantial feature transformation. While blocks 17 and 22 stand out with their high values, the implied importance of these blocks cannot be confirmed by qualitative analysis (Fig. \ref{fig:PixArtBlockImportanceQualitatively}). Furthermore, blocks 1, 2, 4, 27, and 28 are not uniquely identified as critical blocks for the performance of the model, as the values for blocks 19, 20, and 21 are higher, which would mark them as more relevant. As this ranking also fails to align with the qualitative review of the blocks' influence on the final image, the use of representational similarity for block selection in PixArt-$\Sigma$ must also be viewed critically.

Due to the contradictory results of magnitude-based pruning and \gls{CKA}-scores when compared with the qualitative observations, a third methodology was investigated, namely quantifying the importance of individual blocks based on standard performance metrics \cite{FastFlux}. Specifically, the \gls{CLIP}-score and \gls{CMMD} are computed on a small reference dataset consisting of 100 images from the LAION dataset. It should be noted that the standard protocols for computing statistically robust \gls{CMMD} and \gls{CLIP}-scores typically require larger datasets (e.g., upwards of 1,000). However, given the iterative nature of the greedy search (Sec. \ref{sec:GreedyBlockSelection}) and the high computational overhead associated with repeated evaluations, using such a large reference set was prohibitively expensive. Therefore, a sample size of 100 images serves as a sufficient proxy to capture the relative importance ranking of the transformer blocks, providing the necessary directional guidance for the pruning process. As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:PixArtBlockImportanceMetrics} (c and d), both metrics closely align with the qualitative evaluation, confirming that the first four and the last two blocks exert the greatest impact on the final image quality. Consequently, these metrics are selected as the primary criteria for identifying redundant blocks in PixArt-$\Sigma$.
This ranking serves as the foundation for the subsequent, more sophisticated block selection algorithm.

\subsection{Block Selection Algorithms}
\label{sec:PixartBlockSelectionAlgorithms}
A further key challenge after establishing the pruning criteria in model compression is identifying the optimal combinations of blocks to remove simultaneously. When removing only a single block, there are merely 28 possibilities for PixArt-$\Sigma$. However, if the goal is to reduce the model size by $50\%$ necessitating the removal of 14 blocks, the number of possible block combinations escalates to $\binom{28}{14}$. Therefore, it is computationally infeasible to evaluate the \gls{CLIP}-score and \gls{CMMD} for every possible combination.

\subsubsection{Greedy}
A natural approach is to employ a greedy search algorithm. In this method, the importance ranking for all blocks is initially computed, after which the least important block is identified and compressed. Next, the selection criteria, e.g., \gls{CLIP}-scores and \gls{CMMD}, are re-evaluated for the remaining blocks within the newly reduced architecture. By iteratively evaluating and compressing the least important block, the model architecture is progressively reduced while accounting for the interdependencies between blocks. 

\subsubsection{Optuna}
Another approach is to reformulate the block selection problem as a hyperparameter optimization problem using the Optuna framework. The number of blocks to compress, $N_c$, the specific blocks to be compressed, $\{B_i\}_{i=1,\dots,N_c}$, and the compression ratios of each block, $\{ \pi_i \}_{i=1,\dots,N_c}$, can be potential hyperparameters to optimize. Here, the focus is on identifying the blocks $\{B_i\}_{i=1,\dots,N_c}$ given a fixed $N_c$ and  predefined $\{ \pi_i \}_{i=1,\dots,N_c}$ to reduce the search space. As the objective function, the \gls{CMMD} metric is computed on 100 reference images from the LAION dataset. To further speed up the search process, the results of the greedy algorithm are provided as a prior to the Optuna optimization framework, ensuring that the first trials are not picked randomly, which would drastically increase the search time until a good setting is found. 

The Optuna hyperparameter search was tested for the iterative SVD-based pruning strategy with fixed compression ratios $\pi_i = 0.6$ $ \forall i \in \{1, \dots, N_c\}$, where the number of compressed transformer blocks was chosen for each iteration individually. The procedure to determine the least important transformer blocks is as follows. First, the greedy algorithm was leveraged to establish a prior of potentially good candidates for compression. Afterwards, the Optuna hyperparameter framework was applied to refine the block selection. In total, 300 different configurations were evaluated by Optuna, where the decision criterion was solely the \gls{CMMD} score. After the best block combination was identified, the pruned model was fine-tuned. This procedure was executed repeatedly for four iterations. Fig. \ref{fig:AdvantageOfOPtunaPfine-tuning} shows the prior blocks from the greedy algorithm as well as the final block selection of the Optuna algorithm. On the right side, the corresponding \gls{CMMD} values are displayed for each configuration. Note that these represent the \gls{CMMD} values before fine-tuning in order to compare the prior with the final selection. The grey blocks represent the consensus where the Optuna algorithm did not change the blocks from the prior, whereas the yellow and green blocks indicate the blocks exclusively selected by the greedy and Optuna algorithms, respectively. For iterations one to three, one observes that the majority of blocks from the greedy prior were maintained by the Optuna algorithm, only in iteration four do two out of three blocks differ. The large overlap is a first indication that the greedy algorithm already performs well. However, because the greedy result was used as a prior, the Optuna framework search is focused around it, which might also explain the high similarity. Overall, one can conclude that the \gls{CMMD} values obtained by the Optuna algorithm are consistently smaller (better) than the ones obtained by the greedy algorithm. This indicates that at least the pre-fine-tuning performance is enhanced by the refinement step.

Above, it is observed that in each iteration, the Optuna framework was able to identify a block combination for compression which resulted in a smaller \gls{CMMD} value compared to the prior greedy algorithm.

To finally evaluate both the greedy and Optuna approaches, their post-fine-tuning performances were compared. In this step, PixArt-$\Sigma$ was compressed twice, once based on the greedy and once based on the Optuna algorithm, each time targeting the same number of parameters (i.e., the same compression ratios $\pi_i = 0.6$ and the same number of blocks in each iteration). Fig. \ref{fig:AdvantageOfOPtunaPostTraining} shows the corresponding distribution of blocks compressed in each iteration. Please note that in this context "greedy" specifies the compression scheme where the greedy algorithm is strictly utilized as the sole block selection algorithm, in contrast to the previous section, where "greedy" served merely as the prior for the Optuna algorithm.

  An analysis of the block distribution reveals that the early layers are heavily compressed in both schemes, indicating a consensus on the high redundancy of these initial blocks. In the later layers, no clear structure is visible, and the selections diverge. This lack of a consistent pattern suggests that the deeper blocks are largely interchangeable regarding their impact on the model's capacity. 
  
 The evaluation of the fine-tuned compressed models (Fig. \ref{fig:BenchmarksOptuna}) does not provide clear evidence that the Optuna block selection algorithm led to better-performing models post-fine-tuning. Specifically, for the smallest model, the HPSv2 score is even better when using the greedy algorithm, and a similar trend is observed for the second and third iterations regarding the GenEval score. This demonstrates that the fine-tuning process acts as an equalizer, effectively eliminating the small initial advantage of the Optuna algorithm.

\subsection{One-Shot versus Progressive Model Pruning}
\label{sec:OneShot_vs_Progrosseve_Pruning}

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/Experimente/Iterative_vs_Direct/Examples_model_50.pdf}
	\caption{Example images for one-shot versus progressively pruned models by 50\%.}
	\label{fig:Example_OneShot_vs_Progressive}
	\end{figure}



One-shot model pruning compresses the model to its target size in a single step, while progressive pruning employs an iterative scheme of incrementally removing parameters, followed by recovery fine-tuning phases. Both frameworks are tested using otherwise the default settings for all other design choices (Sec. \ref{sec:DefaultSetup}). The number of blocks removed per model and the corresponding number of fine-tuning steps are summarized in Tab. \ref{tab:SettingOneShotvsProgressive}. 

%The exact blocks which are removed for each model can be found in the appendix \ref{sec:OneShotvsProgressiveBlockDistribution} (Fig. \ref{fig:OneShotvsProgressiveBlockDistribution}). 

Fig. \ref{fig:OneShotvsProgressive} displays the performance of one-shot versus progressive pruning on the HPSv2 and GenEval benchmarks across various compression ratios. To ensure a fair comparison, the models for both methods are fine-tuned for the same number of steps and on exactly the same data. Both benchmarks demonstrate that one-shot compression is a viable strategy in the low-compression regime (10\% to 20\% parameter reduction). However, as the compression ratio increases, the progressive approach becomes advantageous because the incremental removal of parameters allows the model to recover its generative capabilities more effectively compared to the abrupt removal of a large number of parameters at once. Fig. \ref{fig:Example_OneShot_vs_Progressive} presents visual evidence that the model progressively pruned by 50\% generates images of higher quality than those obtained using the one-shot approach. Nevertheless, both approaches exhibit noticeable visual degradation at a compression ratio of 50\%.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation Loss}
\label{sec:ExpKDLoss}
In this section, the effectiveness of the individual components of the knowledge distillation loss (Eq. \ref{eq:KnowledgeDistillationLoss}) and their combinations is investigated. In total, five different loss functions are tested: 

 \begin{itemize}
 	\item \textbf{Output-Only Distillation} ($\bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{OutKD}}}$): \\ The \gls{MSE} between the final output of the student and the teacher serves as the sole learning signal.
 	\item \textbf{Standard Distillation} ($\bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{OutKD}}} + \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{FeatKD}}}$): \\ The unweighted sum of the final output loss and the  intermediate feature loss is leveraged as the learning objective.
 	\item   \textbf{Hybrid Distillation} ($\bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{OutKD}}} + \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{FeatKD}}} + \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{Task}}}$): \\ The original diffusion loss is added to the standard distillation loss. 
 	\item \textbf{Normalized Distillation} ($\bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{OutKD,norm}}} + \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{FeatKD,norm}}}$): \\ The final output and the intermediate feature loss are normalized (Sec. \ref{sec:KDLNormalization}) before being summed together to ensure that individual layers do not dominate the total loss. 
 	\item  \textbf{Normalized Hybrid Distillation} ($\bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{OutKD,norm}}} + \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{FeatKD,norm}}}+ \bm{\mathcal{L_\textbf{Task}}}$): \\ This configuration extends the normalized distillation objective by incorporating the original diffusion task loss.
 \end{itemize}
 
 PixArt-$\Sigma$ is pruned to varying sizes between 90\% and 50\% of the original model size utilizing the described loss configurations. The setup follows the exact same settings as described in Tab. \ref{tab:SettingOneShotvsProgressive}.  
 
 Fig. \ref{fig:LossInvestigation} presents the performance of the pruned models obtained by the different loss configurations. According to the HPSv2 benchmark, which measures overall image quality, the normalization of the intermediate features and the final output loss proves advantageous (blue and red lines in Fig. \ref{fig:LossInvestigation}). Both loss configurations that utilize normalization achieve a consistently higher HPSv2 score compared to their unnormalized counterparts. The normalization ensures that the contribution of each intermediate feature to the overall loss is balanced. This prevents the optimization process from prioritizing the later layers, where the errors are numerically larger, to the detriment of the earlier layers. The numerically larger discrepancies between the teacher and the student stem from the larger feature magnitudes in deeper layers, as displayed in Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeIntermediateFeatures}, which presents the \gls{RMS} of the intermediate features after every transformer block in PixArt-$\Sigma$ averaged over 100 prompts. The increase in feature magnitude results from the signal accumulation inherent to the PixArt-$\Sigma$ architecture, which follows the residual formulation $\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \mathbf{x}_l + B_l(\mathbf{x}_l)$, where $B_l$ represents the $l^\text{th}$ transformer block. 
 
 Between the two loss configurations applying normalization, the normalized hybrid distillation performs best. This suggests that incorporating the original diffusion loss alongside the teacher's learning signal is advantageous for the fine-tuning process of the pruned models. The original loss might become critical when the teacher is uncertain itself about a prediction, as it anchors the student to the ground truth. 
 
  The benefit of the original diffusion loss is also confirmed by the unnormalized settings, where the hybrid distillation consistently achieves a higher HPSv2 score compared to the standard distillation approach.
  
  Interestingly, the output-only approach outperforms both standard and hybrid distillation settings for lightly compressed models. This suggests that the unbalanced features prevent effective learning. However, for strongly compressed models, the output-only loss configuration leads to an abrupt drop in quality. This shows that the intermediate feature loss, despite being suboptimally distributed, serves as a critical stabilizer for the fine-tuning process.
  
  The GenEval benchmark largely corroborates the observations from the HPSv2 benchmark, that the normalized hybrid distillation approach performs best overall and that adding the original diffusion loss boosts performance. Notably, the output-only configuration results are even more pronounced, performing comparably well or better than the normalized hybrid distillation approach in the low-compression regime (90\% to 70\%). This suggests that at low compression, removing the constraints of feature matching allows the student to optimize semantic alignment very effectively. However, performance drops severely for larger compression rates. 
  
  Furthermore, it is worth noting that for the high-compression regime of 60\% or 50\%, the unnormalized hybrid distillation approach slightly outperforms the normalized distillation approach. This indicates that in the high-compression regime, the original diffusion loss is critical for maintaining text-to-image alignment.  
  
  
  Fig. \ref{fig:QualitativeLossInvestigation} presents a visual comparison between the five different loss function approaches, where PixArt-$\Sigma$ is pruned to 60\% of its original model size. When comparing the images based on pruning with different loss functions, it becomes clear that the facial contours (columns 1-3, 5) become blurred, especially in the output-only and standard distillation setups. In comparison, the normalized distillation and normalized hybrid distillation show more detail. The latter is also the only method in which both the horse and the astronaut are correctly displayed in the fourth column. With all other methods, only the horse is generated, sometimes with significant artifacts. Here, pruning seems to have weakened the text-image alignment. Overall, these visual examples confirm the values from the performance benchmarks.

 \subsection{Fine-Tuning Protocol}
 \label{sec:fine-tuningProtocol}
 
 After removing blocks from the PixArt-$\Sigma$ architecture, the reduced model was fine-tuned to recover its image generation capabilities. In total, three different fine-tuning protocols were tested to identify the most effective one. In all protocols, the model was trained for 88.5k optimization steps per iteration of the progressive pruning schedule to enable a fair comparison.
 \begin{itemize}
 	\item \textbf{Three-Stage Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
 	This is the default fine-tuning protocol described in Sec. \ref{sec:DefaultSetup}. It is repeated here for the reader's convenience.
 	\begin{enumerate}
 		\item Local Stabilization: The blocks immediately preceding the removed blocks were fine-tuned for 38k steps on the LAION dataset, while all other parts of the model were frozen.while all other parts of the model were frozen.
 		\item Global Optimization: The complete model was optimized for one epoch (38k steps) on the LAION dataset.
 		\item Domain-Specific Refinement: The LAION-PixArt dataset was leveraged to fine-tune the pruned model for 12.5k steps on its teacher's domain.
 	\end{enumerate}
 	
 	\item \textbf{Two-Stage Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
 	In the following protocol, instead of performing local fine-tuning, the entire model was directly fine-tuned for 2 epochs on the LAION dataset.
 	\begin{enumerate}
 		 	\item Global Optimization: The complete model was optimized for two epochs (76k steps) on the LAION dataset.
 		\item Domain-Specific Refinement: The LAION-PixArt dataset was leveraged to fine-tune the pruned model for 12.5k steps on its teacher's domain.
 	\end{enumerate}

 	\item \textbf{Two-Stage LAION only Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
 	The absence of the domain-specific refinement was investigated in this protocol.
 	\begin{enumerate}
 		\item Local Stabilization: The blocks immediately preceding the removed blocks were fine-tuned for 38k steps on the LAION dataset while all other parts of the model were frozen.
 		\item Global Optimization: The complete model was optimized for 50.5k steps on the LAION dataset to ensure the same number of training steps as in the previous fine-tuning protocols.
 	\end{enumerate}
 \end{itemize}

 \label{sec:CompressionStrategyPixart}
 
   	\begin{figure}[H]
 	\centering

 As an alternative to the pruning strategy of removing entire transformer blocks from \gls{DiT}s, low-rank approximation via truncated \gls{SVD} can be utilized to reduce the size of projection matrices within transformer blocks. In PixArt-$\Sigma$, \gls{SVD} compression was applied to the \gls{MLP}, self-attention, and cross-attention matrices (Fig. \ref{fig:PixArt_Sigma_Compresseed_Modules_Architecutre}). To evaluate the efficiency of the \gls{SVD}-based compression against the structural block removal method, a representative operating point was chosen. The rank for the \gls{MLP} was set to $r_\text{MLP} = 512$, corresponding to a compression of approximately $44\%$ of its parameters, and the ranks for the self- and cross-attention matrices were fixed to $r_\text{self-attn} = r_\text{cross-attn} = 128$, representing a parameter reduction of approximately $78\%$. This served as a starting point. Later, the choice of compressing attention matrices more aggressively than the \gls{MLP} matrices is further investigated (Sec. \ref{sec:MLPAttnDiffCompRatios}).

Note that the number of training steps differed significantly, as the default criterion --- a \gls{CMMD} value exceeding 0.1 --- was employed to determine the pruning rate per iteration, consistent with the default settings (Sec. \ref{sec:DefaultSetup}). The observation that \gls{SVD} allows for more parameters to be removed with a less severe impact on the \gls{CMMD} value on the small reference dataset was an initial indication that compression is a more nuanced method than complete block removal. 
  
  Fig. \ref{fig:SVD_vs_Removal} presents the performance of both methods on the HPSv2 and GenEval benchmarks. For both benchmarks, the \gls{SVD}-based compression method outperforms the complete block removal method in both the low- and high-compression regimes. This suggests that distributing the parameter reduction across multiple blocks via compression is less detrimental to model performance compared to removing individual blocks completely. 
  
  For a qualitative comparison of both methods, Fig. \ref{fig:Example_Images_SVD_vs_Removal} provides a set of example images for models compressed to 50\% of their original size, which illustrates the advantage of truncated \gls{SVD} as a compression method. The resulting images contain more details, exhibit fewer artifacts, and more closely resemble the original image. Specifically, column four highlights that the fish is more accurately preserved when applying the \gls{SVD} compression technique. Moreover, column one shows that in high-compression regimes, complete block removal can lead to severe deviations from the original image, in contrast to the \gls{SVD} compression technique. 
   
  In conclusion, the qualitative results confirm the quantitative results of the benchmarks and reveal a clear advantage of \gls{SVD} compression over entire block removal, indicating that most blocks have an independent functional task that is difficult for neighboring blocks to fully recover, but that there are redundancies within the blocks which can be effectively reduced by \gls{SVD} compression.

 \subsection{Sensitivity Analysis of MLP and Attention Layers under \gls{SVD} Compression}
 \label{sec:MLPAttnDiffCompRatios}
 In this section, the \gls{SVD}-based compression strategy was applied, but otherwise the default settings remained. The goal was to investigate how to best distribute the parameter removal within each transformer block. Therefore, individual compression ratios were applied to the \gls{MLP} and attention matrices, whereby both self- and cross-attention matrices were pruned by the same amount. In total, three different scenarios were explored:
 
 \begin{itemize}
 	\item \textbf{Equal Compression}: \\
 	Both \gls{MLP} and attention matrices were compressed by the same percentage, $c_\text{MLP} = c_\text{Attn} = 60\%$. 
 	\item \textbf{Attention-Dominant Compression}: \\
 	The attention matrices were more strongly compressed ($c_\text{Attn} = 80\%$) compared to the \gls{MLP} matrix ($c_\text{MLP} = 40\%$).
 	 	\item \textbf{\gls{MLP}-Dominant Compression}: \\
 	The \gls{MLP} matrix was more strongly compressed ($c_\text{MLP} = 80\%$) compared to the attention matrices ($c_\text{Attn} = 40\%$).
 \end{itemize}
 
 Note that the combined parameter count of self- and cross-attention was approximately equal to the parameter count of the \gls{MLP}. Therefore, all configurations removed a similar number of total parameters from one transformer block. 
 
The scores in Fig. \ref{fig:DistributionCompressionRatios} reveal two distinct trends. The HPSv2 score demonstrates that prioritizing the compression of the attention matrices consistently yields higher image quality than the method focused on compressing the \gls{MLP} matrices. The GenEval benchmark, however, shows that although the attention-dominant approach is advantageous in the low-compression regime, it declines sharply under heavier compression, eventually falling below the \gls{MLP}-dominant approach for models with approximately 400 million parameters. 

 One possible explanation for the divergence between the two benchmarks is the distribution of tasks among the different components of a transformer block. A recent study \cite{dong2025attention} suggests that \gls{MLP}s are crucial for knowledge storage and the processing of visual features. Consequently, strong compression of these layers leads to a significant decrease in image quality (HPSv2: yellow line).
  Fig. \ref{fig:ImagesAttnVsMLP} visually supports the result of the HPSv2 benchmark. The images clearly demonstrate that the \gls{MLP}-dominant approach lacks detail, texture, and structural integrity in the high-compression regime. For instance, at model sizes below 71\%, the \gls{MLP}-dominant approach hallucinates an unprompted headband in the samurai image (column four), and the facial features become distorted at the highest compression ratio. Moreover, for the 60\% model, the burger (column two) is hovering in the air instead of being placed on the table, and the humanoid (column six) is severely deformed. In contrast, the models of the attention-dominant approach, despite being approximately of the same size, do not indicate such severe structural errors, although a general degeneration of image quality at higher compression rates is also visible. 
  
  
 On the other hand, the GenEval benchmark measures text-image alignment. Since the cross-attention mechanism is responsible for integrating the text prompts into the visual latents, its compression in many transformer blocks severely compromises this linking. While the image quality suffers less than with the strong \gls{MLP} compression, the model's text comprehension deteriorates disproportionately. This explains why the attention-dominant approach loses its advantage for heavy compression and is in some cases even worse than the \gls{MLP}-dominant approach. 
 
 The equal compression strategy serves as a baseline compromise between the \gls{MLP}- and attention-dominant approaches. It exhibits results similar to the attention-dominant approach in the high-compression regime, but is slightly worse in the low-compression regime.

 \subsection{Importance-Aware Rank-Adaptive Progressive Model Pruning}
 \label{sec:CompressionSchedulingPixart}
 
 	\begin{figure}[H]
 	\centering
 	\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{images/Experimente/Compression_Schedul/PixartCompressedComponents.pdf}
 	\caption[Example Images SVD Compression Schedule]{50\% models.}
 	\label{fig:PruningSchedule}
 \end{figure}
 
 
In previous experiments, SVD compression was applied to each block at most once. However, restricting compression to untouched blocks may be suboptimal. Incrementally reducing the size of an already compressed block could potentially preserve model performance better than pruning a new dense block. For this reason, the importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive model pruning scheme defined in Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression} was investigated. Assuming that a fixed number of parameters should be removed per iteration, a key design choice is how to distribute them across the model. Parameters can either be distributed across many blocks with light compression, or concentrated on a few blocks with heavy compression. To determine which strategy works better, three different configurations with varying numbers of compressed blocks per iteration were evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{SVD\_7}: Seven blocks were compressed per iteration.
	\item \textbf{SVD\_14}: Fourteen blocks were compressed per iteration.
	\item \textbf{SVD\_21}: Twenty-one blocks were compressed per iteration.
%	\item \textbf{SVD\_28}: All blocks are compressed per iteration.
\end{itemize}
 During this pruning schedule, to determine the importance of blocks of different sizes, the parameter-weighted \gls{CMMD} value was utilized as the block selection criterion.  Within each iteration, 10\% of the original number of parameters were removed. Tab. \ref{tab:PixArtAppendixCompression_iterations} provides detailed information about the base compression ratio and the slope for all three configurations.

For comparison, the \textbf{SVD\_Standard} baseline, where truncated \gls{SVD} was applied at most once per block, was utilized.

Fig. \ref{fig:CompressionSchedule} presents the performance of the evaluated compression schedules on the HPSv2 and GenEval benchmarks.
According to the HPSv2 score, the differences in the low-compression regime are negligible, but they become more pronounced for higher compression ratios. 
 A significant drop for the \textbf{SVD\_7} schedule at a 50\% model size is observed, while the performance of \textbf{SVD\_14} and \textbf{SVD\_21} remains comparable. Furthermore, the \textbf{SVD\_Standard} baseline performs only slightly worse than these two configurations. This suggests that in order to maintain high image quality, it is advantageous to distribute the removed parameters across the model.
 The GenEval benchmark demonstrates that in the high-compression regime, the advantages of \textbf{SVD\_14} and \textbf{SVD\_21} are more pronounced compared to \textbf{SVD\_Standard} and \textbf{SVD\_7}.
 Therefore, it also seems beneficial for text-image alignment to distribute the removed parameters more uniformly across the model. This observation is also qualitatively supported by the example images in Fig. \ref{fig:PruningSchedule}.

 \subsection{Summary of Best Settings for Structural Model Pruning}
 \label{sec:PixArtBestModel}
The results of all experiments establish the following best configuration for structural model pruning:
 
 \begin{itemize}
 	\item \textbf{Block Importance Analysis} \\
 	Sec. \ref{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis} demonstrates that using the \gls{CMMD} value as the primary block selection criterion is highly effective. Particularly in the importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive model pruning scheme, where the number of compressed parameters varies per block, the \gls{CMMD} can be weighted by the number of removed parameters to ensure a fair comparison. 
 	
 	The greedy algorithm performed well enough as a block selection method, such that more advanced methods like Optuna do not provide substantial benefits that would justify the additional compute (Sec. \ref{sec:PixartBlockSelectionAlgorithms}).
 	
 	\item \textbf{Pruning Method} \\
 	Progressive model pruning proves advantageous over one-shot model pruning in the high-compression regime, where models are compressed to below 70\% of their original size (Sec. \ref{sec:OneShot_vs_Progrosseve_Pruning}).
 	
 	\item \textbf{Knowledge Distillation Loss} \\
 	The results indicate that the combination of the original diffusion loss with the normalized feature and output loss leads to the strongest performance in all pruning regimes (Sec. \ref{sec:ExpKDLoss}). 
 	
 	\item \textbf{Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
 	The findings suggest that incorporating the LAION-PixArt dataset into the training procedure is critical, as it contains images of higher quality, demonstrated by the higher HPSv2 and GenEval scores for the three- and two-stage fine-tuning protocols. However, the results regarding the superiority of the three-stage protocol over the two-stage protocol are inconclusive. Based on the benchmark performance, it is not clear if local fine-tuning is beneficial for high-compression regimes (Sec. \ref{sec:fine-tuningProtocol}). However, due to the visually more compelling images from the three-stage approach, it was chosen as the best setting.
 	
 	\item \textbf{Structural Compression Strategy} \\
 	The experiments demonstrate that leveraging truncated \gls{SVD} compression over complete block removal leads to superior performance, even with fewer training steps (Sec. \ref{sec:StructuralCompressionStrategy}).
 	
 	\item \textbf{Sensitivity Analysis of MLP and Attention Layers under \gls{SVD} Compression} \\
 	The sensitivity analysis for \gls{MLP} and attention layers reveals that strong compression of \gls{MLP}s leads to more severe deterioration than heavy compression of attention layers. However, compressing both \gls{MLP} and attention layers equally yields performance comparable to the strong attention compression experiment, but is slightly worse in the low-compression regime. (Sec. \ref{sec:MLPAttnDiffCompRatios}) Consequently, the attention-based compression scheme was adopted for the final configuration.
 	
 	\item \textbf{\gls{SVD} Compression Scheduling} \\
 	The results from Sec. \ref{sec:CompressionSchedulingPixart} suggest that distributing the removed parameters over more blocks (e.g. 14 or 21) is more favorable than distributing them over fewer blocks, which consequently need to be compressed more heavily. As no clearly superior approach between 14 and 21 could be found, 21 blocks were chosen as the best configuration. 
 	
 \end{itemize}
All models resulting from this core progressive \gls{SVD} pruning approach, including the adapted versions for Flux.1-dev in the upcoming section, will henceforth be referred to as \textit{SVDtrunc}.
In the following section, these best practices are applied to Flux.1-dev. Note that due to the different architecture, and therefore potentially different behavior, the sensitivity analysis could not be directly transferred to Flux.1-dev. Additionally, due to the choice of a different dataset, the fine-tuning protocol is also slightly different.
