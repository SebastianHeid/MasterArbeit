\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:EvalMetrics}
For \gls{DM}s, several different metrics have been introduced to measure the overall image quality, the distance between the generated distribution and the true distribution, and the image-prompt alignment. In the following, the standard metrics and benchmarks used in this work are presented: Fr\'echet Inception Distance (\gls{FID}) \cite{FID}, Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (\gls{CLIP}) score \cite{CLIP}, CLIP-Maximum Mean Discrepancy (\gls{CMMD}) \cite{cmmd}, Human Preference Score v2 (\gls{HPSv2}) \cite{HPSv2}, GenEval \cite{ghosh2023geneval} benchmark, and Dense Prompt Graph (\gls{DPG}) \cite{DPG} benchmark.



\subsection{Fr\'echet Inception Distance}
The Fr\'echet Inception Distance (\gls{FID}) \cite{FID} measures the similarity between the model distribution $p_\text{model}$ and the true data distribution $p_\text{data}$. To compare the distributions, a set of images generated by the diffusion model and a reference set from the original data is required. An Inception model, such as Inception-v3 \cite{szegedy2016rethinking}, extracts features for every image in these sets. Under the assumption that the features from both sets follow a multivariate normal distribution, the mean and covariance of the generated image features ($\boldsymbol{\mu}_\text{model}$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\text{model}$) and the features from the original data ($\boldsymbol{\mu}_\text{data}$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\text{data}$) are computed and compared using the Fr\'echet distance \cite{dowson1982frechet}

\begin{equation}
	\text{FID} = \| \boldsymbol{\mu}_\text{data} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_\text{model}\|_2^2 + \text{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\text{model} + \Sigma_\text{data} - 2 \left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\text{model}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\text{data}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}\right) \quad.
\end{equation}

Consequently, lower \gls{FID} values indicate a higher degree of similarity between the generated images and the original images. 

Although \gls{FID} is widely utilized, \cite{cmmd} have identified several shortcomings. First, the underlying Inception-v3 model is trained only on images from 1,000 classes which leads to embeddings that often fail to capture the rich and varied content of the images generated by current diffusion models. Second, the normality assumption for the feature distributions is frequently violated in practice, which can lead to \gls{FID} scores that do not align with human ratings. A third drawback is that the \gls{FID} score does not capture complex distortion, is highly sensitive to the sample size and exhibits bias. 

\subsection{Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training Score}
The Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (\gls{CLIP}) score \cite{CLIP} was developed to measure semantic image-text alignment. To compare an image with a text prompt, two distinct encoders are leveraged, namely a text and an image encoder. They project both inputs into a shared latent space of equal dimensionality. The cosine similarity is then calculated between the text embedding $E_T$ and the image embedding $E_I$
\begin{equation}
	\textbf{sim}(I,T) = \frac{E_I \cdot E_T}{||E_I|| \cdot ||E_T||} \quad.
\end{equation}
where $T$ denotes the text prompt and $I$ the corresponding image. A high score corresponds to a strong text-image alignment. However, \gls{CLIP} struggles with complex compositional prompts and aesthetic preferences, necessitating specialized metrics such as GenEval, and DPG.
\subsection{CLIP-Maximum Mean Discrepancy}
Similar to the \gls{FID}, the \gls{CMMD} \cite{cmmd} score measures the discrepancy between the data and the model distributions. It replaces the Inception-v3 embeddings with \gls{CLIP} embeddings \cite{CLIP}. Since \gls{CLIP} is trained on more images and leverages natural language supervision, its embeddings provides a better representation and capture more semantic details of the images. The features are compared via the maximum mean discrepancy (\gls{MMD}) \cite{MMD1, MMD2}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\text{MMD}^2 &= \frac{1}{m(m-1)} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j \neq i}^{m} k(\mathbf{x}_{\text{data}, i}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{data}, j}) \\
		&\quad + \frac{1}{n(n-1)} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j \neq i}^{n} k(\mathbf{x}_{\text{model}, i}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{model}, j}) \\
		&\quad - \frac{2}{mn} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} k(\mathbf{x}_{\text{data}, i}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{model}, j}) \quad.
	\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_{\text{data}, i} \sim p_{\text{data}}$, $\mathbf{x}_{\text{model}, i} \sim p_{\text{model}}$ and a positive semi-definite Gaussian RBF kernel $k=\exp(- \frac{\| \mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j \|^2}{ 2 \sigma^2})$. By choosing this kernel no distributional assumption over the features is needed and in contrast to the \gls{FID} score, it is an unbiased estimator. Moreover, \cite{cmmd} showed in several ablation studies that the \gls{CMMD} score aligns more closely with human preferences and can detect distortions that the \gls{FID} struggles to identify.   

\subsection{Human Preference Score v2 Benchmark}
The \gls{HPSv2} benchmark \cite{HPSv2} is designed to rank generated images based on human preferences. To model the human preference score, a \gls{CLIP} model \cite{CLIP} is fine-tuned on the HPSv2 dataset \cite{HPSv2}, which contains 434,000 images, generated by nine different text-to-image models or taken from the COCO dataset, along with over 798,000 human preference choices. A human preference choice always includes two images $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$ and a binary ranking $\mathbf{y}$ indicating whether image $\mathbf{x}_1$ is preferred over image $\mathbf{x}_2$, e.g. $\mathbf{y}=[1,0]$ means that $\mathbf{x}_1$ is preferred by the annotator. The human preference score is determined by the fine-tuned \gls{CLIP} model via 
\begin{equation}
	s_\theta (p,\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{E}_\text{txt}(p) \cdot \mathcal{E}_\text{img}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot 100
	\label{HPSv2Score}
\end{equation}

where $p$ is the text prompt, $\mathbf{x}$ is the image, and $\mathcal{E}_\text{img}$ and  $\mathcal{E}_\text{txt}$ are the image and text encoders of the \gls{CLIP} model, respectively. The \gls{HPSv2} benchmark contains four categories, namely ''Animation``, ''Concept-Art``, ''Painting``, and ''Photo`` with 800 prompts each. The prompts are derived from COCO captions \cite{COCO} and DiffusionDB \cite{DiffusionDB}, but cleaned and modified by ChatGPT. A higher score computed with eq. \ref{HPSv2Score} corresponds to better image quality.

\subsection{GenEval Benchmark}
Unlike global metrics such as \gls{CLIP} and \gls{FID}, which struggle to capture the compositional correctness of images, the GenEval Benchmark \cite{ghosh2023geneval} is designed  to evaluate text-to-image alignment based on an object-focused framework. It leverages existing  models, such as an object detection model, a semantic model, and \gls{CLIP}, to analyze the content of the image and determine whether it follows the prompt closely. The benchmark is divided into six categories of varying difficulty:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Single object: A single object is described by the prompt.
	\item Two objects: Two different objects are described by the prompt.
	\item Counting: One object should appear multiple times.
	\item Colors: An object should have a specific color.
	\item Position: Two or more objects should be placed in a certain order relative to each other.
	\item Attribute binding: Correctly associating attributes (e.g. colors) to specific objects (e.g. a red cube and a blue sphere).
\end{enumerate}
For each category, the prompt follows a specific structure. For the benchmark, 553 different prompts are used, and for each prompt, four images with different seeds are generated. The scoring is based on a binary classification determining whether the objects, their quantities, colors, etc. are correctly present in the image (Fig. \ref{Geneval}). This yields a ratio representing the proportion of images that successfully contain all elements described in the prompt.

\subsection{Dense Prompt Graph Benchmark}
The objective of the Dense Prompt Graph (\gls{DPG}) benchmark \cite{DPG} is to evaluate the performance of image generation models on long and detailed prompts. It incorporates existing datasets, such as COCO \cite{COCO}, PartiPrompts \cite{yu2022scaling} and DSG-1k \cite{DSG}  and enriches their prompts using GPT-4 \cite{GPT4} by adding details, attributes, and spatial relations between objects. A fourth dataset, Objects365 \cite{Objects365} is utilized, which was originally developed for object detection models. The various objects in this dataset are organized into super-categories. Typically, one to four objects from the same super-category are randomly sampled, after which GPT-4 generates a dense prompt based on these objects. In total, 1,065 different prompts were collected. 

For evaluation, GPT-4 generates binary questions  following the Dynamic Scene Graph (\gls{DSG}) framework \cite{DSG} which organizes the questions in a hierarchical tree where questions in child nodes can only be true if the questions in their parent nodes are also true. This structure ensures that the answers maintain logical consistency. For example, the parent node normally contains an existence question, e.g. "Is there a motorcycle?" while the child nodes contain questions about its attributes, e.g. "Is the motorcycle blue". Therefore, if there is no motorcycle,also the question regarding the color is rendered obsolete.

 The vision-language model mPLUG-large \cite{mplug} analyzes the generated images with respect to the questions. The evaluation follows a hierarchical aggregation process where the prompt score is defined as the arithmetic mean of all individual question results associated with a specific prompt, while the final DPG benchmark score is calculated by averaging these individual prompt scores across the entire dataset.
