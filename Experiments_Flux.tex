\section{FLUX.1 {[dev]} Compression}
\label{sec:ExpFlux}
FLUX.1 {[dev]} is an 11.9 billion parameter rectified flow model capable of generating detailed, high-quality, and highly aesthetic images. Due to its high parameter count, it is a primary target for model reduction techniques in the literature \cite{FastFlux,HierarchicalPrune,PPCL,Dense2MoE,flux1-lite}. In this section, the compression of FLUX.1 {[dev]} via low-rank approximation of projection matrices within Transformer blocks is investigated. To this end, the insights obtained from previous experiments on PixArt-$\Sigma$ (Sec. \ref{sec:PixArtExp}) are leveraged and assessed whether they hold true for models in the high-parameter regime. Notably, certain experiments were re-evaluated specifically for FLUX.1 {[dev]} to account for its distinct architectural characteristics and significantly larger scale compared to PixArt-$\Sigma$.

First, Sec. \ref{sec:flux_training_details} summarizes the fine-tuning and default compression settings for FLUX.1 {[dev]}.
Second, as with PixArt-$\Sigma$, the qualitative block importance within FLUX.1 {[dev]} is analyzed (Sec. \ref{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}). Third, the training-free performance of \gls{SVD}-based compression is evaluated and compared to complete block pruning and EcoDiff \cite{EcoDiff} (Sec. \ref{sec:trainingFreeCompression}). In addition, Sec. \ref{sec:FluxParameterRemovalDistribution} investigates the distribution of the compression budget over a varying number of blocks modified in each iteration and demonstrates the effectiveness of importance-aware block selection over random or uniform selection. In Sec. \ref{sec:FluxMainResults}, the main results of the proposed compression protocol, \gls{SVD}trunc, and the comparison with competing methods are reported, followed by the application of \gls{SVD}trunc to a step-distilled \gls{DiT}, namely FLUX.1 {[schnell]} (Sec. \ref{sec:FluxSchnellMainResults}). Finally, Sec. \ref{sec:GRASPForDiTs} provides a proof of concept for an additional training-free approach, GRASP (Sec. \ref{sec:GRASP}), which is transferred from \gls{LLM}s to \gls{DiT}s for potential future research.



\subsection{Experimental Default Setup}
\label{sec:flux_training_details}
All experiments were built upon the training and inference code for FLUX.1 {[dev]} and FLUX.1 {[schnell]} published in the following Git repository \cite{kohya_ss_sd_scripts_2024}. The hyperparameters used for fine-tuning FLUX.1 {[dev]} are specified in Tab. \ref{tab:flux_training_config}. For compressing FLUX.1 {[dev]}, the best settings found for PixArt-$\Sigma$ were leveraged with slight adaptions:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Block Importance Analysis} \\
	To determine the block importance in FLUX.1 {[dev]}, every block was individually compressed by 60\%, and a small reference dataset (100 JoyCaption prompts based on the LAION dataset) was utilized to compute the \gls{CMMD} score comparing the images obtained from the original model and the compressed models. Afterwards, the \gls{CMMD} score was weighted by the number of reduced parameters (e.g. a single-stream block contains fewer parameters than a double-stream block). In contrast to the experiments with PixArt-$\Sigma$, due to the high computational demand of FLUX.1 {[dev]}, the scoring was computed in a single initial pass for each iteration, rather than leveraging a dynamic greedy algorithm that would iteratively re-evaluate the remaining blocks after each sequential compression.
	
	\item \textbf{Knowledge Distillation Loss} \\
	The original flow matching loss as well as the normalized intermediate feature and output losses were utilized, with the normalization computed for features from double-stream and single-stream blocks separately
	\begin{equation}
		\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L_\text{Task}} + \lambda_\text{OutKD} \mathcal{L_\text{OutKD}} + \lambda_\text{SingleFeatKD} \mathcal{L_\text{SingleFeatKD}} + \lambda_\text{DoubleFeatKD} \mathcal{L_\text{DoubleFeatKD}} 
	\end{equation}
	with $\lambda_\text{OutKD}  = 10$, $\lambda_\text{SingleFeatKD}=0.01$, and $\lambda_\text{DoubleFeatKD}=0.1$.
	
	\item \textbf{Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
	The complete model was directly fine-tuned on the LAION-FLUX.1 {[dev]} dataset with an image resolution of $1024 \times 1024$ containing a total of 190,000 images. In contrast to PixArt-$\Sigma$, the original LAION dataset was not used, as its quality is significantly lower than that of the LAION-FLUX.1 {[dev]} dataset. Furthermore, since the benchmark results for the three-stage and two-stage fine-tuning protocols were nearly identical, local fine-tuning was omitted.
	
	\item \textbf{Model Compression Strategy} \\
	The low-rank approximation approach via truncated \gls{SVD} was leveraged to reduce the size of FLUX.1 {[dev]} blocks. The individual components within the double-stream and single-stream blocks that were subject to \gls{SVD} compression are displayed in Fig. \ref{fig:ArchDoubleBlockComp} and  \ref{fig:ArchSingleBlockComp}, and their corresponding parameter counts can be found in Tab. \ref{tab:Flux_parameter_count}.
	
	\item \textbf{Compression Schedule} \\
	The importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive compression schedule was utilized to reduce the parameter count of FLUX.1 {[dev]}. Unless mentioned otherwise, 30 blocks were compressed in each iteration and subsequent fine-tuning was performed for 20,000 steps.
	
	\item \textbf{Evaluation} \\
	The reduced models of FLUX.1 {[dev]} were evaluated on the HPSv2 \cite{HPSv2}, GenEval \cite{ghosh2023geneval}, and DPG \cite{DPG} benchmarks.
	
\end{itemize} 

Unless explicitly stated otherwise, these were the settings utilized in the following experiments. To avoid repetition, only the changes compared to these default settings are described in the subsequent sections.



\subsection{Qualitative Block Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}
	
To identify blocks of lower importance within the FLUX.1 {[dev]} architecture, the \gls{CMMD} values weighted by the number of reduced parameters were calculated, as previously discussed in Sec. \ref{sec:MetricBasedSelection}. To obtain a better understanding of the impact of the individual blocks, a qualitative block analysis is performed for FLUX.1 {[dev]}, similar to the analysis conducted in Sec. \ref{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis} for PixArt-$\Sigma$. 

Overall, the double-stream blocks, each containing approximately 340 million parameters, seem to have a greater impact on the image generation capabilities, which may be explained by their higher parameter count. 
Fig. \ref{fig:OverviewCompressionRemovalDouble} presents visual examples of completely pruning (upper row) or compressing these blocks by 60\% (lower row) via truncated \gls{SVD}. In particular, pruning double-stream blocks 1, 2, and 3 proves to be critical, as the image generation capabilities of FLUX.1 {[dev]} completely collapse after their removal. This collapse likely occurs because early blocks are essential for text-image alignment and establishing the global layout structure. Furthermore, the pruning of the last double-stream block leads to a notable shift in lighting, while the content of the image remains intact. In contrast, compressing double-stream blocks seems less critical. Only compressing double-stream block number 3 leads to visible artifacts.

\subsection{Training-Free Model Compression}
\label{sec:trainingFreeCompression}

Fine-tuning FLUX.1 {[dev]} and current \gls{DiT}s is computationally demanding and requires high-end hardware. For this reason, the following section investigates the extent to which compression via low-rank approximation of matrices within Transformer blocks can be applied without subsequent fine-tuning. To this end, the performance of training-free \gls{SVD}trunc compression is compared to complete block pruning and EcoDiff \cite{EcoDiff}, a recently proposed training-free method that masks out neurons within the weight matrices of FLUX.1 {[dev]}. 

The performance of all three approaches was evaluated for models compressed to 90\%, 80\%, and 70\% of their original size. For \gls{SVD}trunc, 30 blocks were modified for each compression ratio using the importance-aware rank-adaptive model compression schedule for determining the compression ratios. Details regarding the base compression ratio and linear decay rate $x$, as well as the specific configurations for the pruning approach, can be found in the Appendix (Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsSVD} and Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsRemoval}).



Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsBenchmarks} summarizes the performance of all three methods on the HPSv2, GenEval, and DPG benchmarks. For slight compression (retaining 90\% of the model's parameter) pruning proves to be most effective, outperforming \gls{SVD}trunc and EcoDiff on all three benchmarks. For instance, it reaches 31.02 on the HPSv2 compared to 29.97 (EcoDiff) and 29.03 (\gls{SVD}trunc). For smaller models (80\% and 70\%), \gls{SVD}trunc demonstrates clear superiority. Especially at a 70\% parameter ratio, the performance of EcoDiff and block pruning completely collapses, while \gls{SVD}-based compression maintains a significant lead across all benchmarks.



Fig. \ref{fig:ExamplesTrainingFree} displays example images generated by the 80\% models, demonstrating the superiority of \gls{SVD}trunc, as its images exhibit finer details and greater aesthetic appeal. Furthermore, its generated content is more closely aligned with the outputs of the original model. For instance, the image in column 5, which shows a horse and an astronaut, depicts a composition that is only successfully reproduced by \gls{SVD}trunc. For further examples of the 90\% and 70\% models, the reader is referred to the Appendix (Fig. \ref{fig:Examples_TrainingFree90} and Fig. \ref{fig:Examples_TrainingFree70}).

\subsection{Preliminary Experiments: Block Selection and Compression Schedule}
This section applies the optimal compression strategies identified for PixArt-$\Sigma$ to FLUX.1 {[dev]} (Sec. \ref{sec:flux_training_details}). Prior to presenting the final results, two preliminary experiments were conducted. First, it is demonstrated that block importance computation on a reference dataset comprising only 100 images proves advantageous over random and uniform block selection. Second, following the methodology in Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression}, the optimal distribution of discarded parameters across the FLUX.1 {[dev]} Transformer blocks is analyzed. 



\subsubsection{Comparison of Block Selection Strategies}
\label{sec:FluxBlockImportanceAnalysis}

	
The image generation process using FLUX.1 {[dev]} is computationally demanding and time-consuming. Consequently, the reference dataset utilized for determining block importance was limited to 100 images. Furthermore, block importance was evaluated only once per iteration, rather than being re-evaluated iteratively as in the experiments on PixArt-$\Sigma$. Typically, image quality metrics are computed on several thousand images to ensure high diversity and statistical reliability. However, \cite{cmmd} demonstrated that \gls{CMMD} is effective even with small sample sizes and is considerably more reliable than \gls{FID}. Thus, the following experiment demonstrates that despite the constrained reference dataset and the single-pass computation of block importance per iteration, the proposed metric-based analysis remains robust and yields superior results compared to random or uniform block selection. 

For random block selection, instead of creating an importance-aware block ranking a fixed number of $k$ blocks were randomly chosen from all available FLUX.1 {[dev]} blocks without distinguishing between double- and single-stream blocks. Based on this random ranking the same compression schema as in \gls{SVD}trunc was applied. In the uniform approach, all blocks within FLUX.1 {[dev]} were compressed at the same compression ratio within each iteration.
Details regarding model sizes and the number of training steps are provided in the Appendix (Tab. \ref{tab:Random_vs_metric_Selection} - \ref{tab:RandomBlockSelectionDetails}). 


The performance over various benchmarks (Fig. \ref{fig:RandomVsMetricBlockSelection}) demonstrates that importance-based block selection, even with a small calibration dataset, leads to superior results compared to a random or uniform baseline. A possible reason is that the \gls{CMMD} score based on 100 images already effectively indicates whether a block is generally suitable for compression or whether it should be retained. For example, as shown in Sec. \ref{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}, the third double-stream block is highly important and was therefore preserved by the metric-based approach, whereas random selection risks compressing it, potentially leading to a significant performance drop.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity Analysis: Compression Breadth vs. Depth}
\label{sec:FluxParameterRemovalDistribution}
The goal of this experiment was to investigate how the discarded parameters should be optimally distributed across the model using the procedure of \gls{SVD}trunc. Three different scenarios were examined in which FLUX.1 {[dev]} was reduced by 10\% in each iteration with a varying number of modified blocks (breadth), and consequently, varying base compression ratios (depth): 

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{20 Blocks}: In each iteration, 20 Transformer blocks were modified.
	\item \textbf{30 Blocks}: In each iteration, 30 Transformer blocks were modified.
	\item \textbf{40 Blocks}: In each iteration, 40 Transformer blocks were modified.
\end{itemize}

Note that the number of blocks does not specify whether double- or single-stream blocks are compressed. This depends entirely on the importance ranking of the Transformer blocks. The base compression ratio, defined as the compression ratio of the least important block, must be adapted across different iterations depending on which blocks are ranked as the least important ones. For example, if in the fifth iteration of the  \textbf{20 Blocks} setting, the algorithm selects already heavily compressed blocks for further compression, a higher base compression ratio must be chosen to achieve the same parameter reduction as in the first iteration. An overview of the values applied in each iteration can be found in Tab. \ref{tab:compression_iterations}. For all experiments, the models were fine-tuned for 20,000 steps in each iteration.

Fig. \ref{fig:FluxParamDistribution} presents the benchmark performances of all three experiments.  For all settings, a significant performance drop is observed when reducing the model size from 60\% (7.14B parameters) to 50\% (5.95B parameters) across all benchmarks.

The \textbf{20 Blocks} setting, which compresses a few blocks heavily in each iteration, performs the worst on the HPSv2 benchmark. In contrast, it demonstrates superior performance on the GenEval (80\% and 70\% models) and DPG (90\% and 80\% models) benchmarks in the low-compression regime, but deteriorates under heavy compression. This  tendency suggests that in highly compressed models, it might be beneficial to distribute the compression effort across several blocks.

The performances of the \textbf{30 Blocks} and \textbf{40 Blocks} settings are closely aligned on the HPSv2 benchmark, with a slight advantage for the \textbf{40 Blocks} configuration. For the GenEval and DPG benchmarks, the \textbf{30 Blocks} setting is superior in the low-compression regime (up to 70\% of the original model size) but drops below the results of the \textbf{40 Blocks} setting under strong compression. 

To summarize, it seems advantageous for text-image alignment (GenEval and DPG) to concentrate the discarded parameters on fewer blocks for mild reduction, but to spread them more uniformly under stricter compression. Furthermore, one observes for the overall image quality (HPSv2) that the heavy compression of only a few blocks is inferior to a more even distribution of the discarded parameters. Consequently, serving as an effective middle ground, \textbf{30 Blocks} is selected as best setting.

\subsection{Main Results: Evaluating SVDtrunc}
\label{sec:FluxMainResults}



In this section, \gls{SVD}trunc is compared to several competing approaches, as detailed in Tab. \ref{tab:CompetitorTable}. Since most competitors compressed their models to approximately 68\% of the original FLUX.1 {[dev]} model size, a corresponding model, \gls{SVD}trunc-m, was evaluated. For this model, in total 49 blocks were compressed and fine-tuning was performed for 40,000 steps (Tab. \ref{tab:sup_training}). 



\gls{SVD}trunc-m nearly matches the performance of FLUX.1 {[dev]} with an averaged degradation ($R$) of only 0.75\%, thereby outperforming all competing approaches. Notably, even \gls{SVD}trunc-s, retaining only 57\% of the original parameters remains highly competitive with a performance degradation of only 2.6\%, surpassing several models with significantly larger parameter counts. While the  \gls{VRAM} consumption of \gls{SVD}trunc-m is slightly higher than that of some competitors, \gls{SVD}trunc-s achieves a superior \gls{VRAM} footprint of 66.9\% relative to the original FLUX.1 {[dev]} model. In summary, \gls{SVD}trunc establishes a new quality-parameter frontier (Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtruncQualityParameterFrontier}), demonstrating both the effectiveness of importance-aware low-rank approximation in \gls{DiT}s and a gradual, predictable degradation of image quality.

A breakdown of all subcategories for HPSv2, GenEval, and DPG can be found in the Appendix (Tab. \ref{tab:sup_hps}, Tab. \ref{tab:sup_geneval}, and Tab. \ref{tab:sup_dpg}).

\subsubsection{Domain Shift}

Gradual domain shifts, e.g. from anime and cartoon-like styles to photorealistic images, are occasionally observed across varying compression stages for \gls{SVD}trunc (Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtruncFlux_Shift}). For instance, in row one, the characteristic blue and white hair, as well as the orange skin, gradually vanish for the compressed models. At parameter retention rates below 68\% of the original model size, the images depict a generic woman who has lost all the distinguishing characteristics of the original subject. Furthermore, in row two, the cartoonish cat is only present until the 80\% stage, thereafter, a realistic cat is generated in the high-compression regime. 

Notably, this domain change does not necessarily coincide with a degradation in image quality, suggesting that in \gls{SVD}trunc, the parameters responsible for the generation of plausible and high-quality images are maintained, while the parameters encoding the speific stylistic elements as specified in the prompts are removed. The reader is referred to the Appendix \ref{sec:Appendix_SVDtrunc} for more examples.

\subsubsection{Memorization Loss}
An analysis of images generated by models compressed to different sizes via \gls{SVD}trunc reveals that even highly compressed models can still generate plausible, high-quality images. However, a progressive loss of conceptual memorization is observed as the compression ratio increases. While moderately compressed models (e.g. 80\% of the original model size) retain highly distinctive concepts, such as the Mona Lisa, the Leaning Tower of Pisa, or specific cartoon characters like Bugs Bunny, highly compressed models (e.g. 57\%  of the original model size) lose this capacity. They instead depict an ordinary woman, a generic building, or a unrecognizable cartoon animal (Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtrunc_Memorization_Loss}). This indicates that \gls{DiT}s store these highly specialized concepts in the parameters deemed unimportant by \gls{SVD}trunc. Consequently, it appears that the substantial parameter count in \gls{DiT}s is essential for memorizing these detailed concepts, whereas only a smaller fraction of the parameters, which likely encode a finite set of visual features and compositional patterns, is sufficient to generate plausible images. Further examples can be found in the Appendix \ref{sec:Appendix_SVDtrunc}.

\subsubsection{Model Efficiency and Latency}
The primary objective of model compression is to reduce the model size. A potential secondary benefit is a reduction in latency due to the reduced parameter count. \gls{SVD}trunc achieves a reduction in \gls{VRAM} as expected, however the measured latency on an NVIDIA A100 GPU decreases only marginally. In fact, for the model retaining 90\% of the original parameters, the execution of one inference step takes even longer (440.04 ms) compared to the inference step of the full model (424.28 ms). The inference latency for \gls{SVD}trunc-m is reduced by only approximately 10\% although 32\% of the parameters were discarded. The reason for this seemingly counterintuitive result, as theoretical FLOPs decreases significantly, is that \gls{SVD} replaces a weight matrix with two low-rank matrices, meaning that instead of one, two matrix multiplications have to be performed. As modern GPUs are highly optimized for large-scale matrix operations, the low-rank matrices need to be sufficiently small to compensate for the overhead of the additional multiplication operation to achieve an actual speed-up. For this reason, a meaningful reduction in inference time is only observed in models that are 70\% or less of the original model size. 

These finding indicates that to substantially reduce the total inference time, \gls{SVD}trunc needs to be combined with step-distillation methods which reduce the number of required inference steps. Therefore, the focus shifts in the next section to the integration of \gls{SVD}trunc with FLUX.1 {[schnell]}, evaluating whether the robustness of low-rank compression persists in a low-step distillation regime.

It is not immediately evident whether \gls{SVD}trunc is effective in this context, as fewer inference steps may imply fewer redundancies within the model. \subsection{Proof of Concept: GRASP for DiTs}
\label{sec:GRASPForDiTs}
In this section, it is demonstrated that \gls{GRASP} \cite{GRASP}, a \gls{SVD}-based method originally developed for \gls{LLM}s, can be leveraged for the training-free compression of \gls{DiT}s, performing even better than ordinary \gls{SVD}. This section serves as a proof of concept that more advanced compression methods from the \gls{LLM} domain are potentially applicable to \gls{DiT}s, presenting a promising avenue for future research. 

 The goal of \gls{GRASP} is to compress \gls{DiT}s via truncated \gls{SVD} as well, but the singular values to retain are identified based on their importance for the image generation task as described in Sec. \ref{sec:GRASP}, rather than solely relying on their magnitude. Here, the performance of \gls{GRASP} is compared to the baseline application of ordinary truncated \gls{SVD} without fine-tuning.
 

This experiment focused solely on the compression of the FLUX.1 {[dev]} double-stream blocks, as they represent the model's most parameter-intensive components. Exclusively for this setup, a pre-existing importance ranking for the FLUX.1 {[dev]} Transformer blocks from \cite{FastFlux} was utilized and in total, four different compression levels were tested. The compression rate for the double-stream blocks was fixed at 76\%, meaning the levels vary solely in the number of blocks subject to compression. For the exact ranks used for \gls{SVD} compression of the individual components, please refer to Tab. \ref{tab:GRASPSVD_ranks}. 

First, the extent to which the gradient-based method \gls{GRASP} selects different singular values compared to the standard magnitude-based \gls{SVD} selection was examined. Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVD} illustrates the importance versus magnitude estimated by \gls{GRASP} for each singular value of the image modulation matrix from the fourth double-stream block. The global view (Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVDa}) reveals a few significant outliers with very high magnitude and importance. Therefore, to investigate the correlation between magnitude and importance for the vast majority of singular values, Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVDb} provides a zoomed-in perspective. Here, one can observe a general positive trend where higher magnitude correlates with higher importance. However, there are numerous singular values with low magnitude but high importance, and conversely, high magnitude values of negligible importance. This discrepancy indicates that \gls{GRASP} might effectively identify important features that magnitude-based methods would miss, potentially leading to an improvement over ordinary \gls{SVD}.

The potential for improvements over ordinary \gls{SVD} compression, as suggested by the analysis of the correlation between magnitude and importance, is confirmed by the evaluation of both methods on the HPSv2, GenEval, and DPG benchmarks (Fig. \ref{fig:GRASPvsSVDBenchmarks}). The results show that models obtained by retaining the most important singular values (\gls{GRASP}), rather than those with the highest magnitude, outperform standard \gls{SVD} across all compression ratios on all benchmarks. Fig. \ref{fig:ImagesGRASPvsSVD} displays example images, demonstrating that the divergence between both methods is particularly pronounced at higher compression rates (e.g. 68\% and 60\% models). \gls{SVD}-based models exhibit significant degradation in image quality and detail at these stages. Furthermore, their text-generation capabilities degrade more severely than those of \gls{GRASP}-based models. For instance, the kangaroo image still correctly displays "Welcome Friends" at the strongest compression for \gls{GRASP}, whereas the \gls{SVD} baseline fails to do so (column 2). However, models compressed with \gls{GRASP} also struggle to generate text reliably at high compression rates (column 4), and severe artifacts emerge at the most extreme compression stage (column 3).  Nevertheless, both image quality and text generation fidelity are better preserved using the \gls{GRASP} method. 

As image details typically represent high-frequency information, these results suggest that high-magnitude singular values primarily encode low-frequency components, while the low-magnitude singular values, which are often discarded by ordinary \gls{SVD}, capture essential high-frequency details. By prioritizing gradients, \gls{GRASP} better preserves these subtle but important singular values, thereby maintaining superior text-image alignment and preserving finer details. 

Future work should investigate whether these results hold true when integrated into the \gls{SVD}trunc framework, and whether, using the singular values obtained via \gls{GRASP} as an initialization for fine-tuning the compressed model yields further performance gains.
