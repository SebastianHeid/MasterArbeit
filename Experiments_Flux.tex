\section{Flux.1-dev Pruning}
\label{sec:ExpFlux}
Flux.1-dev is an 11.9 billion parameter rectified flow model capable of generating detailed, high-quality, and high-aesthetic images. Due to its high parameter count, it is a primary target for model reduction techniques in the literature. In this section, the compression of Flux.1-dev via low-rank approximation of projection matrices within transformer blocks is investigated. To this end, the insights obtained by previous experiments on PixAr-$\Sigma$ (Sec. \ref{sec:PixArtExp}) are leveraged and tested to determine if they hold for models in the high-parameter regime. Notably, certain experiments are re-evaluated specifically for Flux.1-dev to account for its distinct architectural characteristics and significantly larger scale compared to PixArt-$\Sigma$.

First, Sec. \ref{sec:flux_training_details} summarizes the fine-tuning and default compression settings for Flux.1-dev applied after compression.
Second, as for PixArt-$\Sigma$ the qualitative block importance within Flux.1-dev is analyzed (Sec. \ref{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}). Third, the training-free performance of \gls{SVD}-based compression is evaluated and compared to complete block removal and EcoDiff \cite{EcoDiff} (Sec. \ref{sec:trainingFreeCompression}). In addition, Sec. \ref{sec:FluxParameterRemovalDistribution} investigates the distribution of removed parameters over a varying number of blocks and demonstrates the effectiveness of importance-aware block selection over random selection. In Sec. \ref{sec:FluxMainResults} the main results and the comparison with competitors are reported, followed by the application of the method to a step-distilled \gls{DiT}, namely Flux.schnell (Sec. \ref{sec:FluxSchnellMainResults}). Finally, Sec. \ref{sec:GRASPForDiTs} provides a proof of concept for an additional learning-free approach, GRASP (Sec. \ref{sec:GRASP}) which is transfgered from \gls{LLM}s to \gls{DiT} for potential future research.



\subsection{Training Details}
\label{sec:flux_training_details}
All experiments are built upon the training and inference code for Flux.1-dev published in the following git repository \cite{kohya_ss_sd_scripts_2024}. The hyperparameters used for fine-tuning Flux.1-dev are specified in Tab. \ref{tab:flux_training_config}. For pruning Flux.1-dev the best settings found for PixArt-$\Sigma$ are leveraged and specified here again for completeness:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Block Importance Analysis} \\
	To determine the importance of individual blocks in Flux.1-dev, every block is individually compressed by 60\% and a small reference dataset (100 JoyCaption prompts based on LAION datset) is utilized to compute the \gls{CMMD} score. Afterwards, the \gls{CMMD} score is weighted by the number of removed parameters, e.g. a single-stream block contains less parameters than a double-stream block. In contrast to the experiments with PixArt-$\Sigma$, the scoring is computed in a single initial pass for each iteration rather than leveraging a dynamic greedy algorithm that would iteratively reevaluate the remaining blocks after each sequential compression due to the high computational demands of Flux.1-dev.
	
	\item \textbf{Pruning Method} \\
	Progressive structural model pruning is performed.
	
	\item \textbf{Knowledge Distillation Loss} \\
	The original flow matching loss as well as the normalized intermediate feature and output losses are utilized whereby the normalization is computed for features from double-stream and single-stream block seperately
	\begin{equation}
		\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L_\text{Task}} + \lambda_\text{OutKD} \mathcal{L_\text{OutKD}} + \lambda_\text{SingleFeatKD} \mathcal{L_\text{SingleFeatKD}} + \lambda_\text{DoubleFeatKD} \mathcal{L_\text{DoubleFeatKD}} 
	\end{equation}
	with $\lambda_\text{OutKD}  = 10$, $\lambda_\text{SingleFeatKD}=0.01$ and $\lambda_\text{DoubleFeatKD}=0.1$.
	
	\item \textbf{Fine-Tuning Protocol} \\
	The complete model is directly fine-tuned on the LAION-Flux.1-dev dataset with image resolution of $1024 \times 1024$ containing in total 190 000 images. In contrast to PixArt-$\Sigma$, the original LAION dataset was not used as its quality is much lower than for the Flux.1-dev LAION dataset.
	
	\item \textbf{Structural Compression Strategy} \\
	The \gls{SVD}-based compression approach is leveraged to prune Flux.1-dev blocks. The individual components of double-stream and single-stream blocks and their corresponding parameter counts can be found in Tab. \ref{tab:Flux_parameter_count}.
	
	\item \textbf{\gls{SVD} Compression Scheduling} \\
	The importance-aware rank-adaptive progressive pruning scheme is utilized to compress Flux.1-dev. If not mentioned otherwise in each iteration 30 blocks are compressed and subsequently fine-tuning is performed fro 20k steps.
	
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative Block Analysis}
\label{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}
To identify blocks of lower importance in the Flux.1-dev architecture, the \gls{CMMD} values weighted by the number of removed parameters are calculated, as previously discussed in Sec. \ref{sec:MetricBasedSelection}. To obtain a better understanding of the impact of the individual blocks, a qualitative block analysis is performed for Flux.1-dev as in Sec. \ref{sec:ExpBlockImportanceAnalysis} for PixArt-$\Sigma$. 

Overall, the double-stream blocks, each containing approximately 340 million parameters, seem to have a greater impact on the image generation capabilities, which may be explained by their higher parameter count. 
Fig. \ref{fig:OverviewCompressionRemovalDouble} presents visual examples for removing (upper row) or compressing these blocks by 60\% (lower row). In particular, removing double-stream blocks 1, 2, and 3 proves to be critical, because the image generation capabilities of Flux.1-dev completely collapse after their removal. A possible reason is that early blocks are essential for text-image alignment and establishing the global layout structure. In addition, the removal of the last double-stream block leads to a notable shift in lighting, while the content of the image remains intact. In contrast, compressing double-stream blocks seems less critical. Only compressing double-stream block number three leads to visible artifacts.

Fig. \ref{fig:OverviewCompressionRemovalSingle} presents images generated after removing (upper row) or compressing (lower row) a single-stream block comprising approximately 142 million parameters. In most cases, removing a single block does not introduce observable artifacts. The composition of the images changes slightly, but visible artifacts or severe degradations are only observed when removing single-stream block 35, which results in vertical stripes, or block 38, which leads to overly smoothed details. The compression of single-stream blocks by 60\% does not yield to any observable artifacts.

For a complete overview of all removed or compressed blocks please refere to Appx. \ref{appx:FluxBlockImportanceAnalysis}

\subsection{Training-Free Model Compression}
\label{sec:trainingFreeCompression}

	\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/Experimente/Flux/TrainingFree_paper/TrainingFree.pdf}
	\caption[Qualitative Examples Training-Free approaches for Flux.1-dev]{Example images of the 80\% models obtained by EcoDiff, complete block removal and truncated \gls{SVD} compression.}
	\label{fig:ExamplesTrainingFree}
\end{figure}


Fine-tuning Flux.1-dev and current \gls{DiT}s is computationally demanding and requires the latest hardware. For this reason, this section investigates to what extent compression via a low-rank approximation of matrices within transformer blocks can be applied without subsequent fine-tuning. To this end, the performance of \gls{SVD}-based compression is compared to complete block removal and EcoDiff \cite{EcoDiff} which is a recently proposed training-free method that masks out neurons within the weight matrices of Flux.1-dev. 

The performance of all three approaches is tested for models compressed to 90\%, 80\% and 70\% of their original size. For \gls{SVD}-based compression, 30 blocks, ranked by importance, are modified using the linear schedule for compression ratios. Details regarding the base compression ratio and linear decay factor $x$ can be found in the appendix (Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsSVD}) as well as the details for the complete block removal approach (Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsRemoval}).

Tab. \ref{tab:TrainingFreeMethodsBenchmarks} summarizes the performance of all three methods on the HPSv2, GenEval and DPG benchmarks. For slight compression (retaining 90\% of the model) complete block removal proves to be most effective, outperforming \gls{SVD}trunc and EcoDiff on all three benchmarks. For instance, it reaches 31.02 on the HPSv2 compared to 29.97 (EcoDiff) and 29.03 (SVDtrunc). At higher compression rates (80\% and 70\% models), \gls{SVD}-based compression demonstrates clear superiority. Especially at a 70\% parameter ratio, EcoDiff and block removal completely collapse, while \gls{SVD}-based compression leads by a large margin across all benchmarks.

Fig. \ref{fig:ExamplesTrainingFree} displays example images generated by the 80\% models, demonstrating the superiority of \gls{SVD}trunc, as its images exhibit finer details and greater aesthetic appeal. Furthermore, its generated content is more closely aligned with the outputs of the original model. For instance, the image in column 5, which shows a horse and an astronaut, depicts a composition that is only successfully reproduced by \gls{SVD}trunc. For further examples of the 90\% and 70\% models, the reader is referred to the appendix (Fig. \ref{fig:Examples_TrainingFree90} and Fig. \ref{fig:Examples_TrainingFree70}).

\subsection{Importance-Aware Low-Rank Model Compression - Preliminary Experiments}
This section applies the optimal pruning strategies identified for PixArt-$\Sigma$ to Flux.1-dev. Prior to presenting the final results, two preliminary experiments are conducted. First, it is demonstrated that block importance computation on a reference dataset comprising only 100 images proves advantageous over randomly block selection. Second, following the methodology in Sec. \ref{sec:LinearProgressiveCompression} the optimal distribution of removed parameters across the Flux.1-dev transformer blocks is analyzed. 



\subsubsection{Block Importance Analysis}
\label{sec:FluxBlockImportanceAnalysis}

	\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/Experimente/Flux/BlockSelection/Examples_Random_vs_Importance_Block_Selection.pdf}
	\caption[Qualitative Examples Importance and Random Block Selection Flux.1-dev]{Example images of the 49\% models (5.8B parameters) obtained by random, uniform and importance-based block selection.}
	\label{fig:Examples_Random_vs_Importance_Selection}
\end{figure}



The image generation process using Flux.1-dev is computationally demanding and time-consuming. Consequently, the reference dataset utilized for determining block importance is limited to 100 images. Furthermore, block importance is evaluated only once per iteration, rather than being re-evaluated iteratively as in the experiments on PixArt-$\Sigma$. Typically, image quality metrics are computed on several thousand images to ensure high diversity and statistical reliability. However, \cite{cmmd} demonstrated that \gls{CMMD} is effective even with small sample sizes and is considerably more reliable than \gls{FID}. Thus, the following experiment demonstrates that despite the constrained reference dataset and the single-pass calculation of block importance per iteration, the analysis remains robust and yields superior results compared to random block selection. Therefore, the performance between randomly selecting a subset of blocks to prune, uniformly distribute the removed parameters over all blocks and the importance-aware block selection is compared.

In all three experiments, the default training settings (Sec. \ref{sec:flux_training_details}) are applied for four subsequent iterations within the linear progressive pruning framework. Details regarding model sizes and the number of training steps are provided in Tab. \ref{tab:Random_vs_metric_Selection}. 

The performance over various benchmarks (Fig. \ref{fig:RandomVsMetricBlockSelection}) demonstrates that importance-based block selection, even with a small calibration dataset, leads to superior results compared to a random or uniform baseline. A possible reason is that the \gls{CMMD} score based on 100 images already effectively indicates whether a block is generally suitable for compression or if it should be retained. For example, as shown in Sec. \ref{sec:QualitativeBlockAnalysisFlux}, the third double-stream block is highly important and is therefore preserved by the metric-based approach, whereas random selection risks pruning it, potentially leading to a significant performance drop. 

Qualitatively, the performance difference between models obtained by random versus importance-based block selection is illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:Examples_Random_vs_Importance_Selection} and aligns with the quantitative benchmark results. The images generated by the model (5.83B parameters) based on random block selection exhibit artifacts, such as a bear with two heads or a chainsaw with two chains,  while the importance-based approaches preserves the correct semantic structures much better. In addition, the random selection model's text-generation capabilities are inferior (fifth column).

\subsubsection{Sensitivity Analysis: The Role of Parameter Removal Distribution}
\label{sec:FluxParameterRemovalDistribution}
The goal of this experiment is to investigate how the removed parameters should be optimally distributed across the model. Three different scenarios are examined where Flux.1-dev is pruned by 10\% in each iteration with a varying number of modified blocks and consequently, varying base compression ratios: 

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{20 Blocks}: In each iteration 20 transformer blocks are modified.
	\item \textbf{30 Blocks}: In each iteration 30 transformer blocks are modified.
	\item \textbf{40 Blocks}: In each iteration 40 transformer blocks are modified.
\end{itemize}
Note that the number of blocks does not specify whether double- or single-stream blocks are compressed. This depends entirely on the importance ranking of the blocks. The base compression ratio, defined as the compression ratio of the least important block, must be adapted across different iterations depending on which blocks are ranked as the least important. For example if in the fifth iteration of the experimental setting \textbf{20 Blocks}, single-stream blocks and already heavily compressed double-stream blocks are further compressed, a higher base compression ratio is needed to remove the same number of parameters as in the first iteration. An overview of the values chosen for each iteration can be found in Tab. \ref{tab:compression_iterations}. For all experiments, the models are retrained for 20k steps in each iteration.

Fig. \ref{fig:FluxParamDistribution} presents the benchmark performance of all three experiments.  For all settings, a significant performance drop is observed when reducing the model size from 60\% (7.14B parameters) to 50\% (5.95B parameters) of the original model size across all benchmarks. Notably, according to the HPSv2 benchmark, the \textbf{20 Blocks} experiment performs worse at each compression stage than the other two settings. In contrast, its performance on the GenEval benchmark is superior at the 80\% (9.52B parameters) and the 70\% (8.33B parameters) pruning stages. Furthermore, on the DPG benchmark this setting shows superior performance in the low-compression regime but drops below the other experiments in the high-compression regime. Overall, there appears to be a slight tendency suggesting that in highly compressed models, it might be beneficial to distribute the removed parameters more uniformly. However,a definitive conclusion cannot be drawn at this point.

Furthermore, when comparing the \textbf{30 Blocks} and \textbf{40 Blocks} experiments, the results are very similar on the HPSv2 benchmark. For the GenEval and DPG benchmarks, the \textbf{30 Blocks} setting seems to be slightly superior in the low-compression regime and the \textbf{40 Blocks} setting performs better in the high-compression regime. However, a clearly superior setting cannot be determined. However, no single setting is clearly superior across all metrics.

To conclude, the GenEval and DPG benchmarks measure text-to-image alignment, while HPSv2 investigates human preference. It is notable that the \textbf{20 Blocks} and \textbf{30 Blocks} settings perform better in the low-compression regime for GenEval (with the exception of 90\% for \textbf{20 Blocks}) and DPG than the \textbf{40 Blocks} setting. This suggests that for prompt adherence, it is advantageous to concentrate the parameter removal on fewer blocks. In contrast, the results sugges that for the high-compression regime, amore uniform distribution of removed parameters is beneficial.

Regarding HPSv2, the \textbf{40 Blocks} setting seems to perform best for nearly all compression stages which indicates that for overall image quality, a uniform distribution is beneficial.

In this section, the \gls{SVD}trunc method is compared to several competitors, as detailed in Tab. \ref{tab:CompetitorTable}. Most competitors are compressed to approximately 68\% percent of the original Flux.1-dev model size, a corresponding model, \gls{SVD}trunc-m, is evaluated at this compression ratio. For this model the parameter removal is distributed over 48 blocks and fine-tuned for 40k steps. In each further compression iteration, 30 blocks are modified and fine-tuning is performed for 20k steps. 

Tab. \ref{tab:CompetitorTable} shows that \gls{SVD}trunc-m nearly matches the performance of Flux.1-dev with an averaged degradation (R) of only 0.75\%, thereby outperforming all competing approaches. Notably, even \gls{SVD}trunc-s containing only 58\% remains highly competitive with a performance degradatio of only 2.6\%, surpassing several models with significant higher parameter count. While the  \gls{VRAM} consumption of \gls{SVD}trunc-m is slightly higher than some competitors, \gls{SVD}trunc-s achieves a superior \gls{VRAM} consumption of 66.9\%. In summary, \gls{SVD}trunc establishes a new parameter-performance frontier (Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtruncQualityParameterFrontier}) demonstrating the effectiveness of importance-aware low-rank approximation in \gls{DiT}s. 

Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtruncFlux} provides visual evidence of several different compression stages achieved via \gls{SVD}trunc. For models retaining up to 68\% of the original size, the generated images maintain high quality and a high level of detail that coincides with the benchmark performance. For smaller models, the core semantic concepts of the images are preserved but details become increasingly blurred and larger deviations from the original model are observed.

As the long inference time of \gls{DiT}s primarily stems from the iterative denoising process, reducing the number of inference steps leads to a significant speed-up. For this reason, this section demonstrates that \gls{SVD}trunc can also be applied to step-distilled models, specifically Flux.1-schnell, which requires only four inference steps. It is not immediately evident whether \gls{SVD}trunc is effective in this context, as fewer inference steps may imply fewer redundancies within the model. Flux.1-schnell is iteratively compressed, removing 10\% of the original parameters per iteration by modifying 30 blocks, followed by 20k steps of fine-tuning. 

The analysis reveals that compressing Flux.1-schnell to 70\% of its original size results only minor degradation in image quality. Going further down to 60\% of the original model size, the degragation is slightly more pronounced than for Flux.1-dev. When compressing Flux.1-schnell furhter, a significant drop in image quality is observed. This indicates that fewer redundancies are present compared to Flux.1-dev.

Fig. \ref{fig:SVDtruncFluxSchnell} presents visual examples for Flux.1-schnell compressed to different sizes. One observes that from 60\% onwards, the colors become less intense and the image slightly washed out which supports the quantitative trend of the benchmarks.

\subsection{Proof of Concept: GRASP for DiTs}
\label{sec:GRASPForDiTs}
In this section, it is demonstrated that \gls{GRASP} (Sec. \ref{sec:GRASP}) a method originally developed for  \gls{LLM}s can be leveraged for training-free compression of \gls{DiT}s performing even better than ordinary \gls{SVD} compression. This section should serve as a proof of concept that more advanced compression methods from \gls{LLM}s are potentially applicable for \gls{DiT}s which could be investigated further in future works. \\

 The goal of \gls{GRASP} is to compress \gls{DiT}s via truncated \gls{SVD} too but identify the singular values to keep based on their importance for the image generation task rather than solely based on their magnitude as is standard practise in ordinary \gls{SVD} compression. The performance of \gls{GRASP} is compared to the simple application of ordinary truncated \gls{SVD} without re-training on Flux.1-dev for double-stream blocks only. 
 

First, the extent to which the gradient-based method GRASP selects different singular values than the standard magnitude-based \gls{SVD} selection is examined. This experiment focuses only on the compression of the Flux.1-dev double-stream blocks, as they represent the model's most parameter-intensive components. Exclusively for this setup a pre-existing importance ranking for the Flux.1-dev transformer blocks from \cite{FastFlux} is utilized. In total, four different compression levels are tested. The compression rate for the double-stream blocks is fixed to 76\% meaning the levels vary solely in the number of blocks subject to compression. For the exact ranks used for \gls{SVD} compression for the individual components please refer to Tab. \ref{tab:GRASPSVD_ranks}. \\
Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVD} illustrates exemplarily the magnitude versus importance estimated by \gls{GRASP} for each singular value of the image modulation matrix from double-stream block number four. The total view (Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVDa}) reveals a few significant outliers with very high magnitude and importance. Therefore, to investigate the correlation between magnitude and importance for the majority of singulare values Fig. \ref{fig:MagnitudeVsImportanceSVDb} provides a zoomed-in version. Here, one can observe a general positive trend where higher magnitude correlates with higher importance. However, there are numerous singular values with low magnitude but high importance and high magnitude values with negligible importance values. This discrepancy indicates that \gls{GRASP} might effectively identify important features that magnitude-based methods would miss, potentially leading to an improvement over ordinary \gls{SVD}.

The potential for improvements over ordinary \gls{SVD} compression implied by the analysis of the correlation between magnitude and importance is confirmed by the evaluation of both methods on the HPSv2 and GenEval benchmarks (Fig. \ref{fig:GRASPvsSVDBenchmarks}). The results show that models obtained using the most important singular values (\gls{GRASP}) instead of those with the highest magnitude outperform standard \gls{SVD} across all compression ratios on both benchmarks. Fig. \ref{fig:ImagesGRASPvsSVD} displays example images, demonstrating that especially for high compressions (68\% and 60\% models) the divergence between both methods is pronounced. \gls{SVD}-based models exhibit significant degradation in image quality and detail at these compression stages. Furthermore, their text-generation capabilities degrades more severely than those of \gls{GRASP}-based models as the kangaroo image still correctly displays "Welcome Friends" at the strongest compression for \gls{GRASP} but not for \gls{SVD} (second column in Fig. \ref{fig:ImagesGRASPvsSVD}). However, models compressed with \gls{GRASP} also struggles to generate text consistently correctly at high compression (fourth columns) and for the highest compression stage some severe artifacts (third column) can be observed.  Nevertheless, both image quality and text generation capabilities are superior with the \gls{GRASP} method. As image details are typically high-frequency information the results suggest that the high-magnitude singular values primarily encode low-frequency information and the low-magnitude singular values often discarded by ordinary \gls{SVD} store the high-frequency information. By prioritizing gradients, \gls{GRASP} better preserves these subtle but important singular values and, thereby mainiting superior text-image alignment and preserving finer details.
