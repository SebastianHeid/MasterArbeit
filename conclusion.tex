\chapter{Discussion}
This Master's thesis investigated structural model pruning for \gls{DiT}s to enable more efficient image generation and facilitate their deployment in resource-constrained environments, particularly as new models become increasingly large and computationally intensive. An in-depth analysis of a structural pruning framework was conducted on PixArt-$\Sigma$ (Sec. \ref{sec:PixArtExp}), evaluating several design choices and transferring the optimal settings to Flux.1-dev (Sec. \ref{sec:ExpFlux}). Surprisingly, the analysis revealed that low-rank approximation via truncated \gls{SVD} is a highly efficient method for compressing the projection matrices in individual transformer blocks while maintaining strong performance. This contrasts with earlier studies on \gls{LLM}s, which demonstrated that ordinary low-rank approximation led to significant quality loss and required more advanced methods. Therefore, an importance-aware, rank-adaptive progressive pruning approach, \textit{SVDtrunc}, was developed, establishing a new parameter-quality frontier for Flux.1-dev.


In previoius strucutral model pruning methods, model componenets were either removed entirely or replaced by a smaller modul. In contrast, \gls{SVD}trunc leaves the overall architecture untouched and perform a low-rank approximation of weight matrices. Sec. \ref{sec:CompressionStrategyPixart} underlines that low-rank approximation is a strong initialization for compressed models leading to superior results over complete block removal. This demonstrates that the redundancies in \gls{DiT} are primarily within rather than across transformer blocks. It seems that each block has its own functional task and by removing an entire block the neighbouring blocks have to compensate for this potentially limiting their capacity for their own task. Even in a training free-approach (Sec. \ref{sec:trainingFreeCompression}) the \gls{SVD} compressed models outperform EcoDiff \cite{EcoDiff} and the approach off complete block removal for model smaller than 80\% of their original size showing that it preserves the main functionalities for each block.

The analysis further revealed that the task importance-aware block selection approach has been proven beneficial over randomly selecting blocks to prune (Sec. \ref{sec:FluxBlockImportanceAnalysis}). Moreover, instead of compressing each selected block to a specific size, a decreasing linear compression schedule, where less important blocks are compressed more aggressively, has shown superior performance (Sec. \ref{sec:CompressionSchedulingPixart}). These results suggest that different blocks appear to handle tasks of varying complexity, and therefore different blocks can be compressed to different degrees before a performance degradation occurs.

The effectiveness of \gls{SVD}trunc is demonstrated on Flux.1-dev (Sec. \ref{sec:FluxSchnellMainResults}).  \gls{SVD}trunc-m shows near Flux.1-dev performance, only an averaged degradation of 0.75\% over all benchmarks is observed while being compressed to 68\% of its orginal model size. Reducing the model size further the overall benchmark performance drops gradually and predictably such that even \gls{SVD}trunc-s outperforms competing methods of significantly higher model size. 

To truely establish a compression method for efficient \gls{DiT}s the method has to be copatible with step-distilled models, as they require significantly less inference steps. Sec. \ref{sec:FluxSchnellMainResults} demonstrates that for compression ratios up to 30\%, the performance degeneration of Flux.1-schnell is minor ($R=2.72\%$). However, compressing Flux.1-schnell to 50\% of its orignial model size, a significant performance drop occur ($R=10.69\%$). The images appear to be washed out and are of lower quality. This indicates that step-distilled models may have less redundancies in its parameters and therefore cannot be compressed as aggressively as Flux.1-dev. The washed out images are looking similar to images generated by to few steps ordinary \gls{DiT}s. This could also indicate that it would be beneficial to first compress and then apply the step-distillation because the fine-tuning might not be able to recover the specific  properties. 

The investigation of Flux.1-dev uncovered to phenomena. First, occasionally a gradual domain shift, e.g., anime to realistic, images occured for different compression ratios while even strongly compressed models (e.g., up to 39\% of the original size) generate plausible images. This demonstrate that truncated \gls{SVD} is very effective in maintaining paramters for general image generation but the fine details, e.g. specifying the style, are stored in parameters of low-magnitude and are removed and could not be recovered. Second, a memorization loss of highly distinct concepts, e.g., Bugs Bunny, is observed. These phenomena indicate that these highly specialized concepts are encoded in parameters removed by \gls{SVD} and the general ability to generate plausible, good looking images is encoded in a small subset of the parameters. In future work, it could be investigated if it is possible to maintain this details. 

A potentially promising approach is \gls{GRASP} which precisely demonstrated in the training-free case that selecting singular values based on task importance rather than magnitude enables generation of images of greater detail. Therefore, it would be interesting to see if this advantage is also observed after subsequent fine-tuning.
